## 2. Bibliographic identity (confirmed)

**Primary source:**

> Groble, Patricia A. (2018). *Who's in Charge Here? Exercising Authority in the Organization-Volunteer Relationship.* Doctoral dissertation (PhD, Urban Studies and Public Affairs), Maxine Goodman Levin College of Urban Affairs, Cleveland State University. OhioLINK ETD accession `csu1535034198828948`; also archived in CSU EngagedScholarship ETD Archive.

**What the study is:** semi-structured interviews with volunteers and staff at community-serving nonprofits, triangulated against volunteer-handbook analysis. It develops a framework for the authority relationship between organization and volunteer, identifying **three components of the authority relationship and six categories of tools/practices** organizations use to exercise authority over volunteers. Core tension named: volunteers' freedom to choose their work and schedule vs. the organization's need to ensure volunteer activity fulfils mission — the exact tension D4 probes must surface when asking practitioners how they hold volunteers accountable without employment leverage.

**Related peer-reviewed work by the same author (supporting, already-published channel):**

> Groble, P., & Brudney, J. L. (2015). "When Good Intentions Go Wrong: Immunity Under the Volunteer Protection Act." *Nonprofit Policy Forum.* — establishes the liability/accountability boundary conditions around volunteer activity; relevant to D4's accountability sub-domain red-flag clauses (accountability ≠ liability transfer).

## 3. Corroboration against adjacent literature (does the model stand alone?)

A dissertation is admissible on our source list only if its claims are corroborated by peer-reviewed or authoritative practitioner literature. They are:

1. **Rochester's models of volunteer involvement** (service-delivery / support-role / member-activist / co-worker) independently establish that authority relationships between staff and volunteers vary by engagement model — converging with Groble's finding that authority is exercised through differentiated tools rather than a single supervisory mode.
2. **Brudney & Meijs (2019), "More is less? The volunteer stewardship framework and models," *Nonprofit Management and Leadership*** — peer-reviewed framework distinguishing volunteer resource-access and stewardship regimes; corroborates the premise that organizational control over volunteers is structurally constrained and must be exercised through program design rather than positional command.
3. **Hager & Brudney (Urban Institute, *Balancing Act* / volunteer management capacity studies)** — document empirically that accountability and retention practices for volunteers diverge from employee management practice, corroborating Groble's core tension.

**Verdict:** the Groble model is corroborated on all load-bearing claims D4 relies on: (a) volunteer accountability operates without employment authority; (b) organizations exercise authority through identifiable tool categories (expectation-setting, documentation/handbooks, role design, feedback structures, etc.); (c) unmanaged authority tension is a performance-management failure mode. No claim D4 needs from Groble stands on the dissertation alone.
