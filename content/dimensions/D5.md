# Dimension 5 — Recognition, Retention & Culture

**Framework version:** v1.0 · **Draft revision:** D5 v0.1 → audience-tier translation r1
**Status:** CPO-authored draft, awaiting CEO sign-off as domain authority
**Parent issue:** GIV-630 (T2-H → GIV-628 → GIV-620 → GIV-542)

---

## 1. Dimension summary

D5 sustains volunteer commitment through **motivation-matched recognition**, **retention practice grounded in the psychological drivers of stay/leave decisions**, and a **culture of belonging that does not silently rank paid staff above volunteers**. Its second axis — **autonomy vs. over-management** — is shared with D3 (Training, Development & Role Support): the same scoring machinery that detects over-management drift in role support detects it as a retention/culture killer here.

Post-pilot enhancements (psychological-contract literature integration; risk-propensity-differentiated retention) are **out of scope for T2-H** per README §Post-Pilot Enhancements. They sharpen levelling after real pilot data.

## 2. Sub-domains (translation lattice)

| # | Sub-domain | What it tests | Primary literature anchors |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5.1 | Motivation-matched recognition | Whether recognition is chosen by motivation type, not by convention or convenience | Clary & Snyder — Volunteer Functions Inventory (VFI); Grossman & Furano |
| 5.2 | Retention & flight-risk diagnosis | Whether disengagement is spotted early via behavioural signal, not just at exit | Vecina, Chacón & Marzana; Bang & Ross; Bidee et al. |
| 5.3 | Autonomy-preserving support (D3 axis carryover) | Whether support respects volunteer autonomy or drifts into over-management | Deci & Ryan — Self-Determination Theory; Ellis |
| 5.4 | Belonging & staff–volunteer culture | Whether volunteers are integrated as full contributors or treated as second-tier labour | Rogelberg; Studer & von Schnurbein; Netting, Nesbitt & Kettner |

Each band draws 4 probes across these sub-domains, weighted by scope.

## 3. Scope-boundary-awareness rule (v1.0)

Applied framework-wide (see README §Two Scoring Axes):

- **Substantive escalation protects the floor.** A practitioner who correctly recognises a probe is outside their remit and hands off to the right role earns credit at the developmental level of their recognition + handoff, not a penalty for not answering in-band.
- **Uncorrected miscalibration caps a sub-domain at Proficient.** A practitioner who confidently answers a probe substantially outside their scope without noticing they are outside it cannot exceed Proficient in that sub-domain regardless of Expert-level substance elsewhere.
- **Probes and whole sub-domains may thin or drop at lower bands.** Team Lead does not see org-culture policy probes; Director does not see hands-on individual-recognition probes. This is expected. The dimension spine holds because every band gets at least one probe per sub-domain except where scope makes the sub-domain nonsensical for the band (documented per-probe below).

## 4. Autonomy-axis reuse note (from D3)

D3 already ships an autonomy / over-management second axis with validated scoring machinery. D5 reuses it directly for sub-domain 5.3. Concretely:

- The same **strength markers** (S1/S2/S3) that grade evidence in D3 grade evidence here. S1 = concrete instance with observable practice change; S2 = pattern across cases with reasoning; S3 = system/policy design that removes the failure mode.
- The same **red-flag → misconception cap** logic applies: an uncorrected clear over-management pattern caps the autonomy sub-domain at Proficient (§5.5 of the scoring rubric).
- The same **band-floor mechanic** applies: minimal qualifying evidence lifts a band-floor score (3.1 Developing / 5.6 Proficient / 7.6 Expert) into position.

No new scoring code is needed for D5. The engine picks up autonomy-axis probes by sub-domain tag.

## 5. Probes by audience band

Each probe is presented as: **Prompt · Sub-domain · Anchors · Developmental-level anchors (Foundational / Developing / Proficient / Expert) with strength markers · Red-flag / misconception clauses · Scope-boundary-awareness note.**

### 5.1 Team Lead — direct, hands-on leadership; evidence over vocabulary

The Team Lead assesses a small volunteer team. Probes focus on individual recognition, spotting a single volunteer at risk of leaving, resisting over-management in daily support, and welcoming a volunteer into the team without paid-staff/volunteer tiering. Org-culture policy is **out of Team-Lead scope** and does not appear as a probe here.

#### Probe TL-1 — Recognising a team member (5.1 Motivation-matched recognition)

**Prompt.** One of your volunteers, Mira, has quietly delivered six months of reliable work on the same task. Team convention is a Volunteer of the Month shoutout at the next all-hands. Walk through how you would recognise Mira.

- **Anchors:** Clary & Snyder (VFI — six motivational functions: values, understanding, social, career, protective, enhancement); Grossman & Furano (recognition tied to what motivated the volunteer to start).
- **Foundational (1.0–3.0).** Delivers the shoutout because it is the convention; equates recognition with public praise. S1 evidence: names Mira, expresses gratitude, uses the existing channel.
- **Developing (3.1–5.5).** Notices that the shoutout may or may not fit Mira, asks a colleague what Mira might prefer. S1 evidence: some tailoring intent, but no reference to why Mira volunteers.
- **Proficient (5.6–7.5).** Draws on what they know of Mira's motivation (e.g. Mira volunteered for skills-building, not visibility) and matches recognition to it (a written skills reference; a chance to co-lead a session). S2 evidence: articulates the mismatch risk of default public recognition for a values/understanding-motivated volunteer.
- **Expert (7.6–10.0).** Frames recognition as an ongoing motivational-fit conversation, not a one-off act; recounts a prior instance where they changed recognition mode after learning a volunteer's motivation profile shifted; S3 evidence: has a standing lightweight practice for surfacing motivation in 1:1s so recognition decisions are not guesses.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-TL1-a.** Treats recognition as generic thanks decoupled from why the person volunteers. Uncorrected: caps 5.1 at Proficient (misconception cap, §5.5).
  - **RF-TL1-b.** Uses paid-staff HR framing ("Employee of the Month") on volunteers without acknowledging the difference. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap per §5.5.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. A Team Lead who says "I'd escalate recognition policy design to my coordinator" without doing the individual-recognition work is not escalating — they are declining an in-band probe.

#### Probe TL-2 — Spotting a volunteer at risk of leaving (5.2 Retention & flight-risk diagnosis)

**Prompt.** A volunteer who used to arrive early has been arriving late, skipping the team huddle, and going quiet on the group chat. He has not said anything to you. What do you do, and what do you look at first?

- **Anchors:** Vecina, Chacón & Marzana (organisational commitment as retention driver); Bang & Ross (satisfaction/commitment cascade); Bidee et al. (autonomous vs controlled motivation shifts).
- **Foundational.** Waits for the volunteer to say something; assumes life is busy; may thank him at the next session hoping it lifts mood. S1: no diagnostic move.
- **Developing.** Recognises the behavioural change is a signal; asks a well-meaning "everything OK?" without a structured frame. S1: notices, but conflates flight risk with mood.
- **Proficient.** Names the specific behaviours (arrival, huddle, chat) as the signal; opens a low-stakes 1:1 focused on fit and recent role changes; distinguishes life-event disengagement from role-fit disengagement. S2: applies at least one framework (e.g. is this an autonomy/competence/relatedness break in SDT terms).
- **Expert.** Reads the pattern in the context of the team's recent trajectory (a new task, a policy change, a role expansion he did not opt into); has a personal heuristic for behavioural flight-risk indicators tuned to the team; S3 evidence: has changed a team practice after a prior flight-risk episode to reduce the recurrence rate.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-TL2-a.** Treats attendance as the only signal and misses relational disengagement. Uncorrected pattern caps 5.2 at Proficient.
  - **RF-TL2-b.** Assumes turnover is inevitable at volunteer scale — no diagnostic move justified as "he'll leave if he leaves." Red-flag cap.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. Cohort-level flight-risk analysis (multiple volunteers, patterns across roles) is Coordinator scope — a Team Lead who correctly says "I'd raise a cohort trend with my coordinator" earns credit for the escalation.

#### Probe TL-3 — Support without over-management (5.3 Autonomy-preserving support · D3 axis carryover)

**Prompt.** An experienced volunteer, Ana, has been running a weekly session her own way for a year. Your manager wants a standard checklist run before every session. Ana pushes back. How do you handle it?

- **Anchors:** Deci & Ryan (SDT — autonomy, competence, relatedness); Ellis (volunteer supervision as coaching, not control).
- **Foundational.** Enforces the checklist because the manager said so; frames Ana's pushback as compliance failure. S1: no autonomy consideration.
- **Developing.** Softens the delivery, mentions Ana's experience, but still lands on "please do the checklist." S1: notices the autonomy tension, no substantive move.
- **Proficient.** Distinguishes the checklist's protective purpose (safeguarding, safety) from its scope-creep bits; negotiates which items are non-negotiable and which Ana can integrate her own way; documents the outcome so it does not become a repeat conflict. S2: names the autonomy erosion risk explicitly.
- **Expert.** Reframes the manager's ask upstream ("what problem is the checklist solving; is a checklist the right instrument for an experienced volunteer") before the conversation with Ana; treats over-management as a retention risk on a par with under-supervision; S3 evidence: has changed a standing team practice to reduce checklist proliferation after a prior autonomy conflict.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-TL3-a.** Treats volunteer autonomy pushback as a discipline problem. Uncorrected: red-flag cap.
  - **RF-TL3-b.** Treats all volunteers as identical on autonomy needs regardless of experience. Uncorrected: 5.3 cap at Proficient (misconception).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. Policy redesign of the checklist regime is upstream (Coordinator / Program Manager); Team Lead who says "I'll flag this pattern to my coordinator" while still handling this conversation earns escalation credit.

#### Probe TL-4 — Welcoming without tiering (5.4 Belonging & staff–volunteer culture)

**Prompt.** A new volunteer arrives on a day the paid staff are visibly stressed. At break, staff cluster in the kitchen; the volunteer eats her lunch alone in the shared space. What do you do in the moment, and what do you do about it next week?

- **Anchors:** Rogelberg (staff–volunteer integration); Studer & von Schnurbein (organisational culture in nonprofits); Cnaan (volunteer classification and status).
- **Foundational.** Notices, feels awkward, does nothing; frames it as staff being tired. S1: no action.
- **Developing.** Joins the volunteer for lunch that day; does not follow up structurally. S1: individual repair, no systemic move.
- **Proficient.** Joins the volunteer, introduces her to one staff member with a shared interest; raises the pattern with the team the following week as a norm question, not a blame. S2: names the tiering risk.
- **Expert.** Uses the moment as a signal that the team's induction ritual around break/meal culture is under-designed; proposes a small change to induction; S3 evidence: has previously changed a team norm after a similar signal.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-TL4-a.** Rationalises staff tiering as unavoidable ("they're the paid staff, of course they cluster"). Uncorrected: red-flag cap.
  - **RF-TL4-b.** Treats belonging as the volunteer's problem to solve. Uncorrected: 5.4 cap at Proficient.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. Org-wide belonging policy is Director scope; Team Lead flagging the pattern for a program-level conversation earns escalation credit.

### 5.2 Coordinator — day-to-day management; operational proficiency

The Coordinator runs a volunteer pool across shifts and roles. Probes shift from a single volunteer to cohort-level recognition, cohort flight-risk diagnosis, autonomy at the level of recurring complaints from experienced volunteers, and team-culture bridging between paid staff and volunteers as a repeat pattern.

#### Probe C-1 — Recognition programme for a pool (5.1 Motivation-matched recognition)

**Prompt.** You inherited a "years of service pin" recognition scheme. Half the volunteer pool never comes to the annual dinner. What do you change, and how do you justify it to the ED (Executive Director)?

- **Anchors:** Grossman & Furano (recognition-programme design against motivation profile); Clary & Snyder (VFI); Cnaan (heterogeneity of volunteer motivations across a pool).
- **Foundational.** Adds a second event or a nicer pin. S1: convention persists.
- **Developing.** Sends a survey asking volunteers what recognition they would like; acts on modal response. S1: motivation-aware, but treats the pool as homogenous.
- **Proficient.** Segments the pool by motivation profile (values-driven, career-driven, social) and offers a small menu of recognition options that map to segments; justifies to the ED in terms of retention risk of a one-size-fits-all scheme. S2: names the mechanism.
- **Expert.** Frames recognition as a **portfolio** with an evidence base — including that some volunteers actively want no public recognition; runs a small experiment before scaling; S3 evidence: has moved a previous programme from convention-based to motivation-based recognition and can name the retention effect.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-C1-a.** Treats recognition as a fixed budget line disconnected from retention outcomes. Uncorrected: 5.1 cap at Proficient.
  - **RF-C1-b.** Imports employee-recognition frameworks wholesale without adjusting for the absence of a paycheck. Uncorrected: red-flag cap.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe C-2 — Cohort flight-risk diagnosis (5.2 Retention & flight-risk diagnosis)

**Prompt.** Volunteer hours this quarter are flat on the surface but you notice a cluster of veteran volunteers has quietly dropped shifts. New-volunteer intake is masking the loss. What do you do?

- **Anchors:** Vecina, Chacón & Marzana; Bang & Ross; Bidee et al. (autonomous motivation as a leading retention indicator).
- **Foundational.** Reports the healthy total-hours number up; misses the cohort dynamic. S1: no diagnostic move.
- **Developing.** Notices the cohort loss; sends a re-engagement email to lapsed veterans. S1: reactive, not diagnostic.
- **Proficient.** Segments the leavers vs stayers, identifies a common trigger (a recent role change, a policy shift), designs a targeted conversation with three or four representative veterans before acting. S2: distinguishes leading and lagging indicators.
- **Expert.** Treats the cohort dip as a signal that intake growth is disguising a churn problem the ED may not be seeing; frames the retention loss in cost-to-programme terms; S3 evidence: has previously restructured onboarding or role design to close a similar veteran-churn pattern.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-C2-a.** Total-hours as sole retention KPI, no cohort view. Uncorrected: red-flag cap.
  - **RF-C2-b.** Assumes new intake is a substitute for veteran retention. Uncorrected: 5.2 cap at Proficient.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe C-3 — Autonomy pushback as a repeat pattern (5.3 Autonomy-preserving support · D3 axis carryover)

**Prompt.** Three of your team leads have separately raised that their most experienced volunteers are pushing back on new procedural requirements. What does the pattern tell you, and what do you do about it?

- **Anchors:** Deci & Ryan (SDT); Ellis (supervision as coaching); Grossman & Furano (over-management as a retention driver).
- **Foundational.** Frames the team leads' reports as compliance issues; asks the team leads to hold the line. S1: no autonomy read.
- **Developing.** Recognises the pattern is about autonomy, coaches the team leads through conversations. S1: individual coaching, no systemic move.
- **Proficient.** Audits the procedural requirements against protective purpose vs bureaucratic drift; retires or modifies the ones that fail the test; explains to the team leads why. S2: named autonomy mechanism.
- **Expert.** Turns the pattern into a standing check ("what procedural weight are we adding this quarter, and what are we retiring") that resists ratcheting; S3 evidence: has previously killed a checklist or reporting requirement in the name of autonomy preservation and can trace the retention effect.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-C3-a.** Sees only individual-team problems, misses the system pattern. Uncorrected: 5.3 cap at Proficient.
  - **RF-C3-b.** Treats procedural ratcheting as neutral overhead. Uncorrected: red-flag cap.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. Org-wide governance of procedural weight is Director scope; a Coordinator who correctly escalates the recurring pattern for a program/director conversation earns escalation credit.

#### Probe C-4 — Staff–volunteer bridging (5.4 Belonging & staff–volunteer culture)

**Prompt.** A paid staff member has been visibly short with a long-standing volunteer in front of the team. This is the third such incident this quarter, involving two different staff. What do you do?

- **Anchors:** Rogelberg (staff–volunteer integration); Netting, Nesbitt & Kettner (cross-boundary team dynamics); Studer & von Schnurbein.
- **Foundational.** Apologises to the volunteer privately; does not address the staff pattern. S1: individual repair only.
- **Developing.** Raises the incidents with the staff members' line manager one by one. S1: escalation, no synthesis.
- **Proficient.** Names the pattern to the staff line manager and to the volunteer manager together; proposes a joint norm-setting conversation; documents the pattern so it is not re-relitigated. S2: treats it as a shared culture issue.
- **Expert.** Treats the recurrence as a signal of an under-designed staff–volunteer working relationship; proposes a small structural change (shared onboarding moment, joint stand-up, clearer decision rights) as a preventative; S3 evidence: has previously changed a working-relationship norm and can name the effect on volunteer retention or staff satisfaction.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-C4-a.** Treats each incident in isolation. Uncorrected: 5.4 cap at Proficient.
  - **RF-C4-b.** Positions volunteers as "guests" whose dignity is optional. Uncorrected: red-flag cap.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. Org-wide policy on staff–volunteer parity is Director scope; a Coordinator correctly framing this as a program-and-HR conversation earns escalation credit.

### 5.3 Program Manager — cross-team oversight; developing strategic framing

The Program Manager operates above single-team recognition detail and inside program design. Probes shift to recognition strategy across programs, retention analytics and targeted intervention design, role structures that resist over-management drift, and psychological safety and belonging at program scale.

#### Probe PM-1 — Recognition strategy across programs (5.1 Motivation-matched recognition)

**Prompt.** You run three programs with different volunteer profiles: youth mentoring (career-motivated), food bank (values-motivated), and disaster response (episodic, competence-motivated). The ED wants "one consistent recognition programme across the org." Respond.

- **Anchors:** Clary & Snyder (VFI); Grossman & Furano; Ellis (differentiated volunteer management).
- **Foundational.** Complies; designs one programme with slight variants. S1: convention.
- **Developing.** Pushes back weakly on the "one programme" ask; ends up with a compromise that satisfies neither purpose. S1: some awareness.
- **Proficient.** Articulates that a single recognition frame will miscalibrate at least two of the three programs; proposes a shared **principle** (recognition matches motivation profile) with **differentiated instruments** per program; provides evidence from at least one program. S2: separates principle from instrument.
- **Expert.** Reframes the ED's ask as a governance question ("what is 'consistent' protecting — brand? cost? equity? — and can we protect it another way"); shows a small retention-cost estimate of miscalibrated recognition; S3 evidence: has previously defended differentiated program-level recognition to a board or ED and won on the mechanism argument.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-PM1-a.** Collapses under executive pressure without articulating the mechanism cost. Uncorrected: 5.1 cap at Proficient.
  - **RF-PM1-b.** Frames motivation-differentiated recognition as "unfair" rather than fit-for-purpose. Uncorrected: red-flag cap.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe PM-2 — Retention analytics and targeted intervention (5.2 Retention & flight-risk diagnosis)

**Prompt.** You have twelve months of shift, task, and satisfaction-pulse data across your programs. Sketch how you would build a working retention dashboard, and name the first targeted intervention you would run off it.

- **Anchors:** Vecina, Chacón & Marzana; Bidee et al.; Bang & Ross; Grossman & Furano (programme-level retention design).
- **Foundational.** Names hours-per-volunteer as the KPI; treats data as reporting-up material. S1: no diagnostic use.
- **Developing.** Names retention rate and satisfaction pulse; correlates them at a surface level. S1: descriptive analytics.
- **Proficient.** Names leading indicators (shift-cadence change, pulse-score drop, role-change events) and lagging indicators (drop-off); designs a cohort-tracking view; identifies a first intervention (targeted onboarding-plus at month 3) with a hypothesis and a success measure. S2: distinguishes descriptive from causal read.
- **Expert.** Treats the dashboard as a decision instrument, not a report; explicitly names what the dashboard **cannot** tell them (why veterans leave — needs qualitative work); pairs the dashboard with a lightweight qualitative practice; S3 evidence: has previously run a data-informed retention intervention and can name what worked and what did not.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-PM2-a.** Data-worship: treats the dashboard as the truth without qualitative pairing. Uncorrected: 5.2 cap at Proficient.
  - **RF-PM2-b.** Confuses retention rate with engagement quality. Uncorrected: red-flag cap.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe PM-3 — Role structures that resist over-management drift (5.3 Autonomy-preserving support · D3 axis carryover)

**Prompt.** Six months after a role redesign that gave veteran volunteers more autonomy, you notice supervisors are quietly adding oversight steps back in — a check-in here, a report there. What do you do?

- **Anchors:** Deci & Ryan (SDT); Ellis; Grossman & Furano; Cnaan (role definition).
- **Foundational.** Does not notice the drift or does not name it. S1: no read.
- **Developing.** Reminds supervisors of the redesign; expects the drift to reverse. S1: verbal correction.
- **Proficient.** Diagnoses what anxiety is driving the drift (a recent incident, unclear escalation paths, no shared trust signals) and addresses the driver so supervisors do not need to reach for oversight steps. S2: names the mechanism.
- **Expert.** Designs the role so the drift is structurally hard — bright-line decision rights, defined escalation, shared trust signals; treats the drift as a leading indicator of an unresolved fear supervisors have; S3 evidence: has previously redesigned to reduce a similar drift and can name the retention effect.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-PM3-a.** Treats supervisor drift as individual failure rather than system feedback. Uncorrected: 5.3 cap at Proficient.
  - **RF-PM3-b.** Assumes autonomy is a one-time redesign, not a maintained property. Uncorrected: red-flag cap.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe PM-4 — Psychological safety at program scale (5.4 Belonging & staff–volunteer culture)

**Prompt.** A pulse survey shows staff report high psychological safety; volunteers report low psychological safety on the same instrument. Executive reaction is "the volunteers must be misreading the questions." Respond.

- **Anchors:** Rogelberg; Studer & von Schnurbein; Netting, Nesbitt & Kettner; Ellis.
- **Foundational.** Agrees the instrument may be miscalibrated; drops the finding. S1: no defence of the signal.
- **Developing.** Defends the finding but does not know what to do next; escalates and waits. S1: signal preserved, no action design.
- **Proficient.** Names the finding as evidence of a two-tier culture; proposes structured qualitative follow-up with volunteers to identify the specific safety gaps; frames the executive misreading itself as data about the culture. S2: names the tiering mechanism.
- **Expert.** Uses the split as a governance lever ("if we cannot ask this instrument's questions of volunteers, our program design cannot know it is safe for them"); commits the program to closing the split as a KPI; S3 evidence: has previously moved a two-tier culture toward parity and can name what changed.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-PM4-a.** Accepts the "volunteers misread the questions" reframe. Uncorrected: red-flag cap.
  - **RF-PM4-b.** Treats belonging as a soft issue orthogonal to retention. Uncorrected: 5.4 cap at Proficient.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. Org-level culture policy is Director scope; a Program Manager escalating the finding as a governance question earns escalation credit.

### 5.4 Director — org-level design; advocacy; culture and infrastructure

The Director sets recognition philosophy, owns retention as a strategic risk, designs culture at scale, and holds org-wide equity of belonging as policy. Probes drop hands-on individual recognition detail. Instead, they test policy, board narrative, hiring for the culture the org wants, and org-wide belonging as governance.

#### Probe D-1 — Org-level recognition philosophy (5.1 Motivation-matched recognition)

**Prompt.** Draft the two-paragraph section on volunteer recognition for the org's next three-year plan, and defend it to a board member who wants "employee-style annual awards, for consistency."

- **Anchors:** Ellis; Grossman & Furano; Clary & Snyder; Studer & von Schnurbein.
- **Foundational.** Writes generic "we value our volunteers" prose; concedes to the board member. S1: convention.
- **Developing.** Writes principled prose about motivation-matching but concedes on operational instrument. S1: half a position.
- **Proficient.** Distinguishes recognition **philosophy** (motivation-matched) from recognition **instrument** (differentiated across programs); defends the philosophy against the "consistency" ask by naming the retention mechanism; concedes governance guardrails (budget, brand) without conceding the mechanism. S2: separates principle from instrument at policy level.
- **Expert.** Positions recognition philosophy as a **strategic asset** (volunteers are the org's largest labour input; miscalibrated recognition is a foreseeable retention loss); anchors it in the research literature explicitly; S3 evidence: names a governance mechanism (annual review of recognition-fit as part of program review) that keeps the policy honest.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-D1-a.** Imports the employee-recognition frame without adaptation. Uncorrected: red-flag cap.
  - **RF-D1-b.** Treats recognition as an HR line item, not a program design lever. Uncorrected: 5.1 cap at Proficient.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe D-2 — Retention as strategic risk (5.2 Retention & flight-risk diagnosis)

**Prompt.** Present volunteer retention as a strategic risk to the board. You have three minutes. What do you say, and what number do you land on?

- **Anchors:** Vecina, Chacón & Marzana; Rehnborg (volunteer economic value); Ellis; Rogelberg (volunteer-programme sustainability).
- **Foundational.** Presents retention rate; leaves the "so what" implicit. S1: descriptive.
- **Developing.** Presents retention rate and hours-lost; leaves the interpretation to the board. S1: partial framing.
- **Proficient.** Frames turnover as both a **replacement cost** (recruiting + onboarding a new volunteer takes staff time) and a **program-quality risk** (loss of institutional knowledge, weakened service continuity); lands on a specific KPI the board can act on (e.g. veteran retention by cohort). S2: names causal mechanism.
- **Expert.** Frames retention as a **strategic capacity question** ("our program capacity is not a function of intake; it is a function of retained veteran-hours × new-volunteer-hours × integration quality"); shows the board where its strategic decisions (e.g. a scope expansion) will move retention; S3 evidence: has previously moved a board to fund retention infrastructure on this mechanism argument.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-D2-a.** Presents retention as a virtue metric, not a capacity metric. Uncorrected: 5.2 cap at Proficient.
  - **RF-D2-b.** Ignores that veteran turnover degrades program quality even at stable total hours. Uncorrected: red-flag cap.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe D-3 — Culture of autonomy at scale (5.3 Autonomy-preserving support · D3 axis carryover)

**Prompt.** You are hiring a new head of volunteer programs. What do you screen for on the autonomy-supervision axis, and how do you screen for it without the candidate learning to say the "right words"?

- **Anchors:** Deci & Ryan (SDT); Ellis; Grossman & Furano; Studer & von Schnurbein (culture as hire-shaped).
- **Foundational.** Screens on volunteer-management vocabulary; treats fluency as evidence. S1: vocabulary check.
- **Developing.** Uses a behavioural question ("tell me about a time you gave a volunteer autonomy"); accepts self-report. S1: interview mechanics only.
- **Proficient.** Uses a case-based prompt where the candidate must decide whether to add oversight to a working autonomous role; scores their reasoning process, not their conclusion; probes for how they would recognise their own over-management drift. S2: designs the screen against a known failure mode.
- **Expert.** Screens for autonomy as a **standing disposition** (does the candidate resist reaching for oversight instruments under pressure) using a scenario that produces pressure; pairs the hire screen with a first-90-days check on any oversight instruments the new head adds; treats the hire itself as the strongest lever on the culture; S3 evidence: has previously changed a hire specification after an over-management episode and can trace the culture effect.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-D3-a.** Confuses autonomy-supportive vocabulary with autonomy-supportive practice. Uncorrected: 5.3 cap at Proficient.
  - **RF-D3-b.** Screens for autonomy at hire but has no post-hire mechanism to preserve it. Uncorrected: red-flag cap.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe D-4 — Org-wide equity of belonging (5.4 Belonging & staff–volunteer culture)

**Prompt.** Volunteers cannot access the internal Slack workspace where most program conversations happen. IT policy says this is a data-security position. Take a position and act.

- **Anchors:** Rogelberg; Netting, Nesbitt & Kettner; Studer & von Schnurbein; Cnaan (volunteer classification and access).
- **Foundational.** Accepts the IT frame; sets up a volunteer newsletter as a proxy. S1: separate-but-equal drift.
- **Developing.** Escalates the IT decision; ends up with a compromise (volunteers get read-only access to specific channels). S1: half a fix.
- **Proficient.** Names the IT policy as a **belonging-tiering mechanism** dressed as a security position; separates the legitimate data-security concern from the second-tier practice it produces; proposes a design that meets both (e.g. a scoped workspace, volunteer-appropriate roles, an audit trail). S2: names the mechanism.
