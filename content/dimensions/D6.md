## 1. Dimension summary

D6 tests whether a practitioner can navigate the ethics of leading unpaid contributors, ensure equitable access to and treatment within volunteer opportunities, and hold the advocacy voice for the volunteer function — while managing the relational-boundary practice that ethical work with volunteers rests on. D6 is the framework's only **dual-axis** dimension: every probe is scored substantively (Axis S — ethics, equity, advocacy content) *and* relationally (Axis R — Loftus boundary-awareness taxonomy) from the same transcript evidence.

Per `docs/D6-dual-axis-design.md`:

- **Dimension score = min(Axis S aggregate, Axis R aggregate).** Lowest-wins; relational boundary-blindness cannot be bought back with substantive brilliance, and vice versa.
- **No engine change.** Axes surface via sub-domain tagging (`6.1`–`6.4` for substantive, `6.R1`–`6.R6` for relational). RUBRIC_VERSION stays 1.2.
- **Candidate is never told there are two axes.** Dual scoring is an assessor/engine-side lens.

## 2. Sub-domains

### 2.1 Axis S — substantive lattice (`6.1`–`6.4`)

| # | Sub-domain | What it tests | Primary literature anchors |
|---|---|---|---|
| 6.1 | Power, consent & fair treatment in the volunteer relationship | Whether the practitioner recognises the org–volunteer power asymmetry, secures informed consent for what volunteering entails, and treats volunteers fairly under that asymmetry | Studer & von Schnurbein — culture; Rochester — models of involvement; Ellis — practitioner ethics; Groble (2018) — authority-tool ethics |
| 6.2 | Equitable access & inclusive design | Whether volunteer opportunities are designed to be accessible across income, ability, race, caring responsibilities, and time-scarcity — not just open in principle | Rotolo & Wilson — participation stratification; Wilson — volunteer demography; Rochester — model-fit inclusion; Hager & Brudney — capacity constraints on inclusion |
| 6.3 | Data ethics & informed participation | Whether volunteer data (identity, contact, engagement, story) is collected, used, and shared under informed consent, with proportion to purpose | Studer & von Schnurbein — organisational culture; Ellis — data-handling ethics; sector guidance on volunteer data (NCVO / Points of Light where applicable) |
| 6.4 | Advocacy for the volunteer function | Whether the practitioner advocates for the volunteer function inside and outside the org — resources, voice, honest representation — without co-opting volunteer stories | Brudney & Meijs (2019) — stewardship; Rogelberg — programme sustainability; Ellis; Rehnborg — economic-value framing without instrumentalising |

### 2.2 Axis R — relational-boundary lattice (Loftus taxonomy, `6.R1`–`6.R6`)

| # | Category | Failure mode it detects |
|---|---|---|
| 6.R1 | Role–relationship confusion (dual relationships) | Volunteer relationships bleeding into friendship, patronage, or commerce without recognition |
| 6.R2 | Emotional involvement calibration | Over-involvement (rescuing, favouritism) and under-involvement (instrumental detachment) |
| 6.R3 | Gifts, favours & reciprocity | Unexamined reciprocity creating obligation, preference, or perceived corruption |
| 6.R4 | Self-disclosure calibration | Disclosure serving the discloser rather than the relationship's purpose |
| 6.R5 | Confidentiality & information boundaries | Information crossing lines between volunteers, staff, beneficiaries, and public |
| 6.R6 | Power & dependency with beneficiaries | Failing to see the asymmetry volunteers carry into beneficiary relationships |

Axis R is **graded, not binary.** Boundary competence is not "never crosses a line" — it is recognising the boundary, naming the tension, and handling it deliberately. Rigid boundary-policing that damages the relationship is itself a Developing-level pattern; competent practitioners hold boundaries *warmly*.

## 3. Scope-boundary-awareness rule (v1.0)

Applied framework-wide (see README §Two Scoring Axes) and lifted verbatim from D5 r1:

- **Substantive escalation protects the floor.** A practitioner who correctly recognises a probe is outside their remit and hands off to the right role earns credit at the developmental level of their recognition + handoff, not a penalty for not answering in-band.
- **Uncorrected miscalibration caps a sub-domain at Proficient.** A practitioner who confidently answers a probe substantially outside their scope without noticing they are outside it cannot exceed Proficient in that sub-domain regardless of Expert-level substance elsewhere.
- **Probes and whole sub-domains may thin or drop at lower bands.** Every band gets at least one probe per Axis-S sub-domain in D6 — but Axis-R coverage thins by scope (see §5).

Per the D6 design doc §D-6, **escalation credit extends to Axis R**: a practitioner who recognises a relational-boundary situation as beyond what they should handle alone and routes it correctly earns Axis-R credit at the developmental level of that recognition + routing. Relational escalation is Proficient-level relational practice, not avoidance.

## 4. Dual-axis machinery reuse note

Per `docs/D6-dual-axis-design.md` §D-2, no engine change is needed. Relational-axis evidence is recorded as signals tagged with relational sub-domain ids (`6.R1`–`6.R6`), parallel to substantive sub-domain ids (`6.1`–`6.4`). Standard §5 machinery — position formula, band floors (3.1/5.6/7.6), §5.5 red-flag caps, §5.5 3-signal IE minimum with the BUG-001 ruling (all signals count) — applies per axis. **Dimension score = min(Axis S aggregate, Axis R aggregate).** RUBRIC_VERSION stays 1.2.

## 5. Probes by audience band

Each probe is presented as: **Prompt · Sub-domain · Anchors · Developmental-level anchors (Foundational / Developing / Proficient / Expert) with strength markers · Relational-axis note (R-active probes) · Red-flag / misconception clauses (axis-tagged -S or -R) · Scope-boundary-awareness note.**

### 5.1 Team Lead — direct, hands-on leadership; evidence over vocabulary

The Team Lead assesses a small volunteer team. Probes focus on consent and fair treatment in a single volunteer relationship, inclusive practice inside the team, handling volunteer information one shift at a time, and (5.4) an early advocacy instance. R-load is on the practitioner's own boundary practice in direct relationships.

#### Probe TL-1 — Consent and fair treatment on shift (6.1 Power, consent & fair treatment)

**Prompt.** A volunteer, Amara, mentioned she is uncomfortable with a task on her shift that involves door-to-door contact — she was not told this was part of the role when she signed up. Walk through the conversation and what you do this week.

- **Anchors:** Studer & von Schnurbein — practitioner-facing ethics culture; Ellis — practitioner ethics; Groble (2018) — expectation-setting overlap.
- **Foundational (1.0–3.0).** Tells Amara this is what the role is; asks her to try it once more. S1: no consent read.
- **Developing (3.1–5.5).** Acknowledges the mismatch, offers to swap her out this week, does not surface the systemic issue (the role description misled her). S1: named for this instance, not resolved.
- **Proficient (5.6–7.5).** Names the mismatch as a consent failure by the org, not by Amara; offers her a real choice (swap this shift; reshape her role; leave with no penalty); flags to coordinator that the role description was misleading. S2: distinguishes fair-treatment ethics from operational convenience.
- **Expert (7.6–10.0).** Uses the moment to check what else the shift is asking of volunteers that wasn't in the consent-at-signup; asks the coordinator whether the role description gap is one-off or a pattern; S3: has previously turned a consent gap into a durable role-description change and can trace the effect.
- **Relational axis (6.R6 Power & dependency).** The prompt sits inside a beneficiary-facing task (door-to-door). Relational reading: F — no view of Amara's or the resident's asymmetry ("just knock"); D — vaguely uneasy about the beneficiary interaction but does not name it; P — names both power asymmetries (org's over Amara; Amara's over doorstep residents) and calibrates the ask against both; E — treats the double asymmetry as design information for the shift, not just a case handled once.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-TL1-a-S.** Treats Amara's discomfort as reluctance to be managed. Uncorrected: caps 6.1 at Proficient (exceptionalism-adjacent).
  - **RF-TL1-b-R.** Presses Amara to "just try it" without recognising the beneficiary-side asymmetry either. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.R6 (boundary-blindness).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. Rewriting role descriptions is Coordinator-plus; flagging the gap while handling the conversation earns escalation credit on both axes.

#### Probe TL-2 — An access request from a volunteer with a caring role (6.2 Equitable access & inclusive design)

**Prompt.** A volunteer, Kenji, asks whether he can bring his eight-year-old daughter to his weekly two-hour shift on the food-parcel line because his usual childcare has fallen through. Your standard rota policy is "no non-volunteers on shift." Answer him this week and think through what it says about the shift.

- **Anchors:** Rotolo & Wilson — caring-responsibility stratification; Rochester — models-of-involvement fit; Ellis; Hager & Brudney.
- **Foundational.** Refuses on the policy; treats the request as noncompliance. S1: policy over person.
- **Developing.** Makes a one-off exception this week; does not surface what the request reveals. S1: humane instance, no systemic read.
- **Proficient.** Handles this week — either a safe accommodation (a child-friendly corner of the site, or Kenji taking a swap he can cover) or an honest "not this week, and here's what we can do so it doesn't cost you your shift"; names to the coordinator that the "no non-volunteers on shift" policy is quietly filtering out carers; separates safeguarding constraints (real) from operational-convenience preferences (renegotiable). S2: distinguishes access ethics from rule enforcement.
- **Expert.** Reads the request as evidence about who the current shift design excludes; ties the design gap to the specific under-represented group; S3: has previously changed a shift-design constraint to widen access and can name the effect on cohort composition.
- **Relational axis (6.R1 Role–relationship confusion, 6.R2 Emotional involvement calibration).** Relational reading: F — either bends the whole rule out of empathy for Kenji (over-involvement, rule collapses for anyone the practitioner likes) or holds the rule coldly (under-involvement); D — sees the tension but resolves it as personal favour ("just this once because I like you"), which sets up dual-relationship expectation; P — holds the rule and the person together, without turning the accommodation into a private arrangement with Kenji; E — treats consistent, transparent handling of similar requests as boundary infrastructure (documented, not remembered case-by-case).
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-TL2-a-S.** Frames the access barrier as Kenji's problem to solve. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.2 (equity-blindness).
  - **RF-TL2-b-R.** Makes a private favour of the accommodation ("don't tell the others"). Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.R1 (boundary-blindness / exceptionalism edge).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. Redesigning access policy is Coordinator-plus; escalation credit for flagging the systemic filter while still handling Kenji this week.

#### Probe TL-3 — A volunteer's disclosure on shift (6.3 Data ethics & informed participation)

**Prompt.** During a shift, a volunteer, Priya, tells you in confidence that she has been in treatment for depression and that's why she's been quiet lately. Your coordinator asks the next day, "Everything OK with Priya? She seems off." What do you do — this week and if the coordinator asks again next week?

- **Anchors:** Ellis — practitioner ethics; Studer & von Schnurbein — culture on disclosure; sector guidance on volunteer-data proportionality.
- **Foundational.** Tells the coordinator what Priya said, treating it as helpful context. S1: no consent read.
- **Developing.** Deflects vaguely ("she'll be OK") without going back to Priya. S1: partial move, no principled position.
- **Proficient.** Holds Priya's confidence; tells the coordinator only what Priya has consented to share (or, if unclear, "she's dealing with something personal; she'd rather I not go further"); goes back to Priya to check what, if anything, she wants raised, and offers her the option of a light adjustment; separates *care for Priya* from *information about Priya*. S2: named proportionality-to-purpose; separates duty-of-care from data flow.
- **Expert.** Uses the moment to notice that the team has no clear norm about what stays in a volunteer's confidence and what does not; suggests a light norm to the coordinator (in general terms, not naming Priya); S3: has previously helped a team make an implicit disclosure norm explicit and can name the trust effect.
- **Relational axis (6.R5 Confidentiality & information boundaries).** Relational reading: F — passes the disclosure on as gossip-adjacent detail; D — holds it this once but has no principle; P — holds Priya's confidence and offers her control of what is shared; E — makes the confidentiality practice legible so it doesn't rest on one person's discretion.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-TL3-a-S.** Treats the disclosure as safeguarding-reportable when it is not. Uncorrected: caps 6.3 at Proficient (proportionality failure).
  - **RF-TL3-b-R.** Shares the specifics with the coordinator uninvited to "help." Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.R5 (boundary-blindness).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. If Priya's disclosure suggests she is at risk to herself or others, that is a safeguarding move, not a confidentiality-holding move — routing that correctly (to the coordinator / safeguarding lead) is escalation credit on Axis R and Axis S both; conflating the two is a scope-and-relational failure at once.

#### Probe TL-4 — First advocacy instance for the volunteer function (6.4 Advocacy for the volunteer function)

**Prompt.** In a staff meeting, a paid staff member says, "The volunteers are a nice-to-have but honestly they slow us down." Your coordinator is not in the room. Do you respond in the moment? How, and what do you do afterwards?

- **Anchors:** Brudney & Meijs (2019) — stewardship framing; Ellis; Rogelberg — programme sustainability language for practitioners.
- **Foundational.** Says nothing; complains privately later. S1: no advocacy move.
- **Developing.** Defends volunteers emotionally ("that's not fair!") without a substantive rebuttal; does not raise it with the coordinator. S1: named, not framed.
- **Proficient.** Responds in the moment with one calibrated example (a specific thing the team's volunteers do that the paid staff-member's work depends on), holds the tone at "let me push back on that" not "you're wrong"; afterwards raises the interaction with the coordinator so it doesn't sit as private folklore. S2: separates advocacy-as-defence from advocacy-as-story-of-mechanism.
- **Expert.** Reads the comment as a signal about staff–volunteer culture, not just this staff member; offers the coordinator both what happened and what it suggests about how volunteers are talked about across the team; S3: has previously raised a culture signal to a coordinator that led to a change and can name the effect.
- **Relational axis.** R-silent — the ethical work is Axis-S advocacy and staff-relationship handling, not one of the six relational-boundary categories in a load-bearing way.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-TL4-a-S.** Weaponises a specific volunteer's story to make the point ("well, Fatima puts in more hours than you do"). Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.4 (advocacy-as-story-mining, plus the volunteer did not consent to be used in the argument).
  - **RF-TL4-b-S.** Concedes silently to protect the working relationship; treats advocacy as a director's job. Uncorrected: caps 6.4 at Proficient (advocacy-vacuum).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. Formal advocacy narrative for the org's board is Program-Manager-plus; a Team-Lead who handles the moment and hands the culture signal up owns the band correctly.

### 5.2 Coordinator — day-to-day management; operational proficiency

The Coordinator runs a volunteer pool across shifts and roles. Probes shift from a single volunteer relationship to policy across the pool, coaching team leads on ethics moves, cohort-level equity reads, first-line handling of gifts/dual-relationship instances, and light standing advocacy. R-load shifts to pattern management and coaching.

#### Probe C-1 — A dual-relationship situation across the pool (6.1 Power, consent & fair treatment)

**Prompt.** You discover that one of your volunteers, Yannis, has been quietly paying another volunteer, Sabine, cash to cover his shifts when he cannot make them. Both say it's a private arrangement between friends. Handle this — this week and structurally.

- **Anchors:** Studer & von Schnurbein — culture; Groble (2018) — authority-tool ethics; Ellis.
- **Foundational.** Ends the arrangement, reprimands both; nothing changes structurally. S1: rule enforced, no ethics read.
- **Developing.** Ends the arrangement, has an awkward conversation, does not surface what the situation reveals. S1: partial move.
- **Proficient.** Names the two overlapping problems: (a) the org's consent-at-signup did not extend to a cash-for-shifts market; (b) a hidden dual-relationship (paid-substitution) is forming inside a volunteer pool. Ends the arrangement, clearly and without shame; names why (fair-treatment across the pool, not moral wrong-doing); asks Yannis what would actually solve his availability problem (swap system, reduced hours); asks Sabine whether the money mattered or the shifts did. S2: named the org-consent gap and the peer-power gap.
- **Expert.** Reads it as a signal about the shift-covering system (people are paying for a workaround that should exist openly); designs or advocates for a transparent swap board; S3: has previously turned a shadow arrangement into a legitimate system and can name the effect.
- **Relational axis (6.R1 Role–relationship confusion, 6.R3 Gifts/reciprocity).** Relational reading: F — treats it as a rule breach; D — sees the dual-relationship but not the reciprocity/obligation Sabine has taken on; P — holds both the role–relationship confusion (paid-substitution ≠ volunteering) and the reciprocity/obligation on Sabine; E — closes the informal market by legitimising the underlying need, not by policing the workaround.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-C1-a-S.** Treats it as a personnel issue solely with Yannis. Uncorrected: caps 6.1 at Proficient (fair-treatment failure toward Sabine).
  - **RF-C1-b-R.** Frames the situation as fine "because they're both adults." Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.R1 / 6.R3 (exceptionalism).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe C-2 — Cohort-level equity read on retention data (6.2 Equitable access & inclusive design)

**Prompt.** Your six-month retention data shows that volunteers from your two largest recruitment channels drop out at very different rates: the university partnership retains at 65 %, the community-partnership channel at 22 %. Both cohorts started with similar numbers. What do you do?

- **Anchors:** Rotolo & Wilson — participation stratification; Wilson — demography; Rochester — model fit; Hager & Brudney.
- **Foundational.** Concludes the university channel is "better volunteers" and doubles down there. S1: reads the gap as a supply signal.
- **Developing.** Notices the gap is uncomfortable, does not change action; says the two groups are hard to compare. S1: named, not investigated.
- **Proficient.** Names the gap as a design signal, not a volunteer-quality signal; investigates likely mechanisms (shift times that clash with paid-work hours; social language and dress norms in the room; whether the community cohort ever gets to the "core" roles or is parked in peripheral ones); tests one change; tracks the retention delta. S2: separates demography-of-recruitment from experience-in-role.
- **Expert.** Refuses to draw the "better volunteers" conclusion as an ethics move — it would reproduce structural exclusion under the language of merit; states that refusal explicitly to the ED if pressed; S3: has previously named a merit-language equity failure inside their org and can trace the effect on cohort composition.
- **Relational axis (6.R2 Emotional involvement calibration, 6.R6 Power & dependency).** The relational read is at the level of the coordinator's stance toward the two cohorts: F — invests emotionally in the retained cohort, coldly with the drop-out cohort ("they weren't really committed"); D — sees the emotional-investment imbalance but treats it as personal; P — recognises that emotional-involvement calibration across a demographic gap *is* a structural equity move, not just an interpersonal one; E — designs against the imbalance by making the emotional-investment moves observable (welcome protocols, check-ins) so they don't rest on the coordinator's warmth toward familiar volunteers.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-C2-a-S.** Reads the retention gap as a merit gap. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.2 (equity-blindness / meritocratic default).
  - **RF-C2-b-R.** Concentrates coaching and shift-choice attention on the retained cohort. Uncorrected: caps 6.R2 at Proficient (under-involvement with the drop-out cohort as pattern).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. Program-level recruitment redesign is PM-plus; escalation credit for surfacing the delta with a proposed one-shift change and asking the PM for the broader review.

#### Probe C-3 — Volunteer story request from communications (6.3 Data ethics & informed participation)

**Prompt.** Your communications team asks you to send them "three or four moving volunteer stories" to use in an appeal campaign. They want quotes and photos. What do you send them, and what do you refuse to send?

- **Anchors:** Ellis — data ethics; Studer & von Schnurbein; sector volunteer-data guidance.
- **Foundational.** Sends their names, quotes, and photos from what you have on file. S1: no consent read.
- **Developing.** Asks the volunteers verbally; treats the ask as a small favour; does not distinguish appeal-campaign use from routine internal use. S1: consent-in-passing.
- **Proficient.** Asks each volunteer explicitly what they are being asked (appeal campaign, their name and story, external distribution, indefinite duration on the org website); explains they can decline without any effect on their volunteering; captures the consent in writing; sends only what they have consented to and only for the use they have consented to. S2: named consent-to-use scope; refuses to send anything about a volunteer who says no or gives an ambiguous answer.
- **Expert.** Recognises that a story-request campaign is a recurring pattern; sets up a simple standing consent form volunteers can update; refuses to over-index on stories from more articulate / more socially confident volunteers because it distorts what the org's volunteer face looks like; S3: has previously changed a story-consent practice and can name the trust effect.
- **Relational axis (6.R5 Confidentiality & information boundaries).** Relational reading: F — treats volunteer stories as org property once obtained; D — asks each time but without a stable norm; P — treats consent as ongoing and revocable; E — designs the practice as boundary infrastructure that survives staff turnover.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-C3-a-S.** Sends photos or quotes without documented consent. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.3 (consent-vacuum).
  - **RF-C3-b-R.** Uses volunteers' openness with the coordinator as *de facto* consent to publish. Uncorrected: caps 6.R5 at Proficient (boundary-blindness on relational-to-public leap).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe C-4 — Advocacy inside the org: rota conflict with paid programme (6.4 Advocacy for the volunteer function)

**Prompt.** The paid programme team wants to move volunteer shifts to accommodate a new grant deliverable. The change would double parking cost and add a 40-minute commute for your community-cohort volunteers. Make the case for the volunteer function without derailing the programme team's grant.

- **Anchors:** Brudney & Meijs (2019); Rogelberg; Ellis.
- **Foundational.** Refuses the change on principle; frames it as staff vs. volunteers. S1: adversarial default.
- **Developing.** Accepts the change; tells the community-cohort volunteers to make it work; does not name the equity cost. S1: capitulation.
- **Proficient.** Frames the trade-off in terms the programme team can hear: the shift change will reduce retention in the community cohort, which will (a) increase recruitment cost, (b) reduce the demographic diversity the grant probably assumes, (c) create a gap the grant hasn't budgeted to backfill; proposes an alternative or a phased move; makes the equity claim in the same breath as the operational claim. S2: yokes advocacy to the programme team's own interest.
- **Expert.** Uses the negotiation to install a standing check ("volunteer-experience impact" as one line in programme change decisions); does not need to win this one to win the standing check; S3: has previously turned a single advocacy negotiation into a durable decision-hook and can name the effect.
- **Relational axis.** R-silent — this is Axis-S advocacy and interpersonal handling, not a load-bearing R-category.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-C4-a-S.** Treats the paid programme team as the adversary. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.4 (advocacy-as-warfare).
  - **RF-C4-b-S.** Absorbs the change silently to keep the peace. Uncorrected: caps 6.4 at Proficient (advocacy-vacuum).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. Board-level advocacy on volunteer-function share of resource is Director scope; escalation credit for handing the standing-check idea up to the PM.

### 5.3 Program Manager — cross-team oversight; developing strategic framing

The Program Manager operates above single-team detail. Probes shift to consent design across programmes, equity architecture in recruitment and role design, data-governance for volunteer information, and standing advocacy where the programme design meets the ethics. R-load shifts to policy design and making relational-boundary practice legible.

#### Probe PM-1 — Consent design across programme redesign (6.1 Power, consent & fair treatment)

**Prompt.** You are redesigning one of your programmes and existing volunteers' roles will change substantively — new tasks, new location, different rhythm. What do you owe them, and how do you handle those who don't want the new shape?

- **Anchors:** Studer & von Schnurbein — culture; Rochester — model-fit consent; Ellis; Groble (2018).
- **Foundational.** Announces the new roles; volunteers who don't like it can leave. S1: no consent read.
- **Developing.** Announces the change; offers vague accommodation; does not name that a substantial role change requires re-consent. S1: partial move.
- **Proficient.** Frames the redesign as a material change that requires re-consent from each affected volunteer: (a) full description of the new role, (b) explicit right to decline without penalty, (c) offer of a suitable alternative role or a dignified exit, (d) honest acknowledgement that the org is asking for their agreement, not announcing it. S2: named material-change threshold; treats declining as legitimate, not resistance.
- **Expert.** Uses the redesign to sharpen the org's ongoing consent practice (what counts as a material change that triggers re-consent; who decides); documents the decision so it isn't re-litigated case-by-case; S3: has previously turned a difficult redesign into a durable re-consent norm and can name the trust dividend.
- **Relational axis (6.R2 Emotional involvement calibration, 6.R6 Power & dependency).** Relational reading: F — treats resistance as under-commitment; D — takes long-service volunteers' distress personally and softens the redesign inconsistently; P — holds both the redesign necessity and the affected volunteers' legitimate loss (of the role they signed up for) without collapsing either; E — uses the org's power over the change deliberately, not silently.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-PM1-a-S.** Treats consent-at-signup as covering any future role. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.1 (consent-freeze).
  - **RF-PM1-b-R.** Applies the redesign warmly to long-service volunteers, coldly to newer ones. Uncorrected: caps 6.R2 at Proficient (calibration failure by tenure).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe PM-2 — Gifts and self-disclosure policy at programme level (6.2 Equitable access & inclusive design)

**Prompt.** Volunteers in your programmes work in close proximity to beneficiaries: some receive food, cards, and small gifts. Some volunteers over-share their personal circumstances with beneficiaries. Neither has a policy. Draft one, and defend the design to the ED who wants "a clear gift-and-self-disclosure code of conduct."

- **Anchors:** Ellis — practitioner ethics; Studer & von Schnurbein; Rochester.
- **Foundational.** Bans gifts and personal disclosure across the board. S1: rigid rule.
- **Developing.** Writes a gift-value cap and a "keep it professional" line; does not distinguish contexts. S1: partial policy.
- **Proficient.** Distinguishes (a) gifts that create obligation vs. incidental gestures within a proportionate cap; (b) self-disclosure that serves the volunteer–beneficiary relationship vs. self-disclosure that serves the volunteer; sets a light disclosure requirement for gifts received; treats the policy as a *guide-and-log*, not a rule-and-punish; makes the escalation path clear (when to raise with coordinator). S2: named the two failure modes (obligation, self-serving disclosure).
- **Expert.** Refuses the "clear code of conduct" framing where it would replace judgement with rule-count; explains to the ED that boundary practice is graded — rigid coding produces the boundary-as-wall failure — and offers a *decision-support policy* instead: named categories, worked examples, supervision channel; anchors in equity — beneficiaries from different backgrounds will read gifts differently, and volunteers from different backgrounds will read the disclosure norm differently, so the policy has to hold that variance without becoming vague; S3: has previously refused a coding push and can name the effect.
- **Relational axis (6.R3 Gifts, favours & reciprocity, 6.R4 Self-disclosure calibration).** Relational reading: F — policy either bans everything or nothing; D — sets a rule but treats it as external compliance; P — designs the policy so it makes graded practice legible; E — treats the policy as boundary infrastructure that supports practitioners rather than replacing their judgement.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-PM2-a-S.** Applies a middle-class gift-cap that reads as accusation in some cultural contexts. Uncorrected: caps 6.2 at Proficient (equity-blindness).
  - **RF-PM2-b-R.** Bans all self-disclosure. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.R4 (boundary-as-wall).
  - **RF-PM2-c-R.** Leaves disclosure entirely to individual discretion with no supervision channel. Uncorrected: caps 6.R4 at Proficient (boundary-blindness).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe PM-3 — Volunteer data governance for a new CRM (6.3 Data ethics & informed participation)

**Prompt.** The org is moving to a new CRM that will hold volunteer identity, engagement, shift history, disclosures, and beneficiary interactions in one record. IT and the ED both want "richer volunteer records" for retention analysis. What do you push for and what do you refuse?

- **Anchors:** Ellis — data ethics; Studer & von Schnurbein; sector guidance on volunteer data proportionality.
- **Foundational.** Accepts the design; assumes the org's existing consent covers it. S1: no proportionality read.
- **Developing.** Raises concerns loosely; ends up with almost the full design shipped. S1: partial pushback.
- **Proficient.** Named principles: (a) proportion-to-purpose — only fields the org can name a use for; (b) separation of routine engagement data from sensitive disclosures (disability, health, safeguarding) which need stricter access and shorter retention; (c) volunteer's right to see and correct their record; (d) retention periods per data type. Refuses a design where sensitive disclosures sit in the same access tier as shift history. S2: named the proportionality principle and the sensitivity-tier design.
- **Expert.** Refuses the "richer record" framing where richness is not tied to a specific decision; reframes as "the smallest record that supports the decisions the org actually makes about volunteers"; ties this to trust as a strategic asset (volunteers who trust the org's data handling stay; a data-handling failure damages recruitment as much as retention); S3: has previously reshaped a data-collection design and can name the effect.
- **Relational axis (6.R5 Confidentiality & information boundaries, 6.R6 Power & dependency with beneficiaries).** Relational reading: F — treats data as neutral records; D — sees the confidentiality risk but not the power dimension; P — recognises that beneficiary interactions logged against a volunteer's name touch the beneficiary's data ethics too; E — designs governance so beneficiary and volunteer confidentiality are load-bearing at the same tier, and volunteers cannot become searchable proxies for beneficiary data.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-PM3-a-S.** Aggregates volunteers' sensitive disclosures into "richer retention analytics." Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.3 (consent-vacuum via scope-creep).
  - **RF-PM3-b-R.** Logs beneficiary detail against volunteer records without separate beneficiary consent. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.R5 / 6.R6 (boundary-blindness with real safeguarding risk).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. Where the CRM design surfaces a safeguarding-adjacent data question the PM has not been asked to handle alone (e.g. disclosure of vulnerable-adult beneficiary detail), escalating to the safeguarding lead and legal counsel is Proficient-level *scope and relational* escalation, not avoidance.

#### Probe PM-4 — Standing advocacy in programme planning (6.4 Advocacy for the volunteer function)

**Prompt.** In your next programme-planning cycle, the volunteer function is being folded into "programme delivery capacity" with no separate line for volunteer investment (recruitment, coordination, recognition, infrastructure). Advocate for what the volunteer function needs to remain healthy, without making it about you.

- **Anchors:** Rogelberg — programme sustainability; Brudney & Meijs (2019); Rehnborg — honest economic-value framing; Ellis.
- **Foundational.** Argues for more budget in general terms. S1: no mechanism.
- **Developing.** Argues from the volunteer function's importance in principle; doesn't tie to programme outcomes. S1: partial argument.
- **Proficient.** Ties volunteer-function investment to the programme outcomes the plan already commits to: without X of coordination capacity, retention falls, and the programme delivers Y% less of its target; presents a small, decision-forcing line-item; separates *volunteer-function overhead* from *volunteer effort* honestly (does not present volunteers as "free"). S2: named mechanism; refuses to instrumentalise volunteers as free labour to win the argument.
- **Expert.** Names the pattern (volunteer-function investment absorbed into programme cost) as a governance risk to the volunteer function's sustainability; offers a light standing measure (annual volunteer-function health check as part of programme review); refuses to overstate the volunteer economic-value figure to win the budget line — treats overclaim as long-term damage to the advocacy voice; S3: has previously won a standing measure rather than a single-cycle budget bump.
- **Relational axis (6.R4 Self-disclosure calibration).** Relational reading: F — advocacy framed as personal wanting; D — argues for the function without noticing it reads as self-interested; P — recognises the "your own budget" frame the argument sits in and disarms it explicitly ("this is not a case for my role, it's a case for the programme's volunteer capacity"); E — makes the argument in a way that would still stand if the current PM left tomorrow.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-PM4-a-S.** Overclaims volunteer economic value to win the argument. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.4 (advocacy-overclaim, sibling of D4's impact-overclaim family — same failure mode, advocacy side).
  - **RF-PM4-b-S.** Instrumentalises volunteers ("we get all this for free") to make the case. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.4 (advocacy-that-degrades-what-it-defends).
  - **RF-PM4-c-R.** Frames the ask around personal reputation or workload. Uncorrected: caps 6.R4 at Proficient (self-serving disclosure).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

### 5.4 Director — org-level design; advocacy; policy and culture

The Director sets the philosophy for the org's ethics with volunteers, owns equity as a governance commitment, and holds the advocacy voice at board and sector level. Probes drop hands-on operational detail. R-load shifts to boundary *culture and infrastructure* — governance of relational practice, not its enactment.

#### Probe D-1 — Volunteer ethics as governance framing (6.1 Power, consent & fair treatment)

**Prompt.** Draft the two-paragraph section on volunteer ethics for the org's three-year plan, and defend it to a board member who wants "the same ethics code we use for staff, applied consistently across the org."

- **Anchors:** Ellis; Studer & von Schnurbein — culture; Groble (2018); Rochester.
- **Foundational.** Writes generic language ("we treat volunteers with respect"); concedes to the board member. S1: convention.
- **Developing.** Writes principled prose; concedes on the "one code" instrument. S1: half a position.
- **Proficient.** Distinguishes the ethical duties the org owes volunteers as a distinct class of contributor — informed consent about what volunteering entails, fair treatment under power asymmetry, dignified exit, honest use of their contribution and voice — from the employment-ethics frame (which turns on the wage relation and is therefore mis-shaped for volunteers); concedes governance guardrails (safeguarding, equal treatment, complaint channel) without conceding the shared-code instrument. S2: named structural distinction; positioned as fit-for-purpose, not soft.
- **Expert.** Positions the volunteer-ethics section as a strategic asset (an org whose volunteer-ethics is clearly distinct from and better-fit than its staff-ethics is a better place to volunteer, which shows up in recruitment and retention); ties the section to the org's mission narrative; anchors it in research literature (Ellis, Studer & von Schnurbein, Groble); S3: names a governance mechanism (annual review of volunteer-ethics coherence as part of the programme review) that keeps the section honest and prevents HR-import drift.
- **Relational axis (6.R1 Role–relationship confusion, 6.R6 Power & dependency with beneficiaries).** Relational reading: F — no view of relational-boundary practice as a governance concern; D — includes a "professional conduct" line without model; P — names dual-relationships and beneficiary-power practice as governance-owned, with a supervision channel; E — treats relational-boundary infrastructure (disclosure routes, supervision, gift log) as part of the ethics section, not a soft add-on.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-D1-a-S.** Imports the staff ethics code without adaptation. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.1 (employee-mechanic import on the ethics axis).
  - **RF-D1-b-R.** Treats relational-boundary practice as an individual-conduct question outside board oversight. Uncorrected: caps 6.R1 at Proficient (boundary-blindness at governance level).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe D-2 — Equity as governance commitment (6.2 Equitable access & inclusive design)

**Prompt.** The board's diversity data on volunteers shows the org's volunteers are 78 % white, 71 % over 55, and 68 % female — in a service area whose beneficiary community is racially and generationally different. The chair wants to know if this is a problem and if so what the board should commit to.

- **Anchors:** Rotolo & Wilson; Wilson; Rochester; Studer & von Schnurbein; Ellis.
- **Foundational.** Says the org's volunteer base "reflects who volunteers"; treats the gap as external. S1: no governance read.
- **Developing.** Says the gap matters, proposes a targeted recruitment push; doesn't examine the ways the org filters. S1: partial move.
- **Proficient.** Frames the gap as a governance-level equity concern with two mechanisms — (a) supply-side stratification (who has the time, transport, cultural comfort, and social invitation to volunteer) and (b) org-side design (shift times, language of the ask, role hierarchies, who volunteers see themselves reflected by inside the org); commits the board to concrete design changes on (b) rather than only recruitment pushes on (a); accepts that closing the gap is slow and refuses to overclaim short-term wins. S2: separates supply and design mechanisms; refuses a merit-language frame.
- **Expert.** Names the risk of two governance failure modes: (i) treating diversity as a *representation* problem (adding token volunteers from the beneficiary community into peripheral roles) rather than a *belonging and power* problem; (ii) treating the gap as a communications problem for the recruitment channel rather than an ethics problem for the role architecture; commits the board to a *standing* equity review of the volunteer function (not a one-off audit); S3: has previously reframed a diversity conversation from representation to belonging at governance level and can name the effect.
- **Relational axis (6.R2 Emotional involvement calibration, 6.R4 Self-disclosure calibration).** Relational reading: F — no view of relational boundaries as culture-level; D — treats relational culture as a soft variable; P — recognises that a warm-but-monocultural volunteer culture reads to under-represented volunteers as *exclusion by warmth* (the in-group's ease is the out-group's signal), and that this is a boundary-calibration failure at culture level, not an individual friendliness question; E — designs relational-boundary norms so they hold across demographic difference without asking under-represented volunteers to do the accommodation.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-D2-a-S.** Treats the demographic gap as reflecting who "wants to volunteer." Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.2 (equity-blindness at governance level).
  - **RF-D2-b-R.** Assumes cultural warmth of the existing volunteer base is universally read as welcome. Uncorrected: caps 6.R2 at Proficient (calibration-by-in-group).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe D-3 — Data ethics and the gift-and-conflict register at board level (6.3 Data ethics & informed participation)

**Prompt.** Legal counsel is asking for a volunteer gifts-and-conflicts register that the board can inspect. The Chief Executive wants a lighter touch: "we can trust our people; don't create bureaucracy." Position the two paragraphs of the annual report that address volunteer data ethics and the register.

- **Anchors:** Ellis; Studer & von Schnurbein; sector guidance on volunteer data; Groble & Brudney (2015) — G2 accountability–liability adjacency.
- **Foundational.** Sides with the Chief Executive; light-touch prose. S1: convention.
- **Developing.** Compromises on a register that exists but is not really used; annual-report language is vague. S1: partial move.
- **Proficient.** Sets up the register as decision-support, not surveillance: a light standing log of gifts received above a proportionate threshold, disclosed dual-relationships, and conflicts of interest — accessible to the board through a summary, not a name-list; frames it as protecting volunteers as much as the org (a documented gift is one that cannot later be reframed by someone acting in bad faith); the annual-report language explains why the register exists in terms of trust and protection, not audit and surveillance. S2: named the protective function; refused the surveillance framing.
- **Expert.** Ties the register to the org's data-ethics posture more broadly (proportion-to-purpose, sensitivity tiers, retention) and to the accountability–liability boundary from D4 (a documented gift or conflict does not itself create liability — the point is decision-support, not risk transfer to the volunteer); refuses both extremes (heavy audit register; no register at all) with a mechanism argument; S3: has previously landed a register at board level that changed a specific decision and can name the effect.
- **Relational axis (6.R3 Gifts, favours & reciprocity, 6.R5 Confidentiality & information boundaries).** Relational reading: F — treats the register as a compliance object; D — treats it as informal; P — treats it as boundary infrastructure that supports practitioners; E — designs the register so a volunteer whose entry appears knows why and can see their own entry — confidentiality within the org, not from the volunteer.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-D3-a-S.** Publishes named volunteer entries in the annual report without consent. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.3 (consent-vacuum).
  - **RF-D3-b-R.** Designs the register so volunteers cannot see their own entry. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.R5 (boundary-as-wall inverted — using confidentiality as opacity).
  - **RF-D3-c-R.** Treats the "no register" position as a virtue signal of trusting volunteers. Uncorrected: caps 6.R3 at Proficient (boundary-blindness, exceptionalism-adjacent).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. Where a specific register entry surfaces a matter beyond board governance (e.g. suspected fraud), routing correctly to legal counsel and safeguarding is Proficient-level *scope and relational* escalation, not evasion.

#### Probe D-4 — Advocacy voice: org voice vs. beneficiary voice (6.4 Advocacy for the volunteer function)

**Prompt.** The org is preparing a public campaign on a policy issue that affects both the beneficiary community and the org's programmes. Volunteers have both direct experience (some are from the beneficiary community) and views. The Chief Executive wants a "unified org voice." Set the position on whose voice speaks in what part of the campaign.

- **Anchors:** Brudney & Meijs (2019) — stewardship at advocacy level; Ellis; Studer & von Schnurbein; Rogelberg.
- **Foundational.** Adopts the "unified org voice"; volunteers are recruited to endorse the org line. S1: co-optation default.
- **Developing.** Acknowledges the tension; volunteers are quoted where convenient. S1: partial move.
- **Proficient.** Separates three voices explicitly: (a) the org's institutional voice — its policy analysis and asks; (b) the beneficiary community's voice — not for the org to speak on their behalf, only to platform where consent and structure allow; (c) the volunteer function's voice — practitioners' front-line experience, which can support (a) without being conscripted into it; sets consent gates for each; refuses to have volunteers front the campaign as *if* they were beneficiaries or as *if* the beneficiary community had endorsed the org line. S2: named three-voice distinction; treats advocacy ethics as separate from advocacy effectiveness.
- **Expert.** Positions advocacy voice architecture as a governance question, not a communications question; sets a standing rule the board owns ("volunteers are not deployed as beneficiary proxies; beneficiary voice is not aggregated by the org without explicit structural consent"); accepts the campaign may be less punchy for it and treats that as a legitimate ethics cost worth paying; S3: has previously refused a campaign design at board level and can name the reputational effect.
- **Relational axis (6.R4 Self-disclosure calibration).** Relational reading: F — collapses volunteers' personal experience into org marketing; D — asks volunteers to share stories case-by-case without a standing frame; P — treats each volunteer's disclosure decision as their own, disaggregated from org strategy; E — designs the advocacy structure so relational-boundary practice at the volunteer/beneficiary interface is preserved through the campaign, not eroded by it.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-D4-a-S.** Uses volunteers as unpaid endorsement labour for an org policy position. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.4 (advocacy-as-co-optation).
  - **RF-D4-b-S.** Aggregates beneficiary voice through volunteer channels without beneficiary consent. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.4 (voice-arrogation).
  - **RF-D4-c-R.** Pressures volunteers to disclose personal experience for campaign use ("we really need your story"). Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap on 6.R4 (self-disclosure serving the org, not the volunteer).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. This probe is Director-owned because it requires policy authority, board-facing communication, and voice-architecture design in the same move.

## 7. Red-flag / misconception summary

Every red flag is axis-tagged in its id (`-S` scope/substantive, `-R` relational). Caps apply per axis; the D6 dimension score is `min(Axis S aggregate, Axis R aggregate)`.

### 7.1 Substantive red-flag families (Axis S)

- **Consent-vacuum.** Data collection, use, or disclosure that outruns what the volunteer has been asked and has agreed to. RF-TL3-a-S (safeguarding-report mis-thresholding), RF-C3-a-S, RF-PM1-a-S, RF-PM3-a-S, RF-D3-a-S. Sibling to D5's employee-import family only in that both are HR-frame drift; distinct in that this is a consent, not an incentive, failure.
- **Equity-blindness / meritocratic default.** Reading demographic and access gaps as merit or supply signals rather than design signals. RF-TL2-a-S, RF-C2-a-S, RF-PM2-a-S (culturally mis-calibrated policy), RF-D2-a-S.
- **Advocacy-vacuum / advocacy-degradation.** Not making the advocacy case, or making it in a way that instrumentalises volunteers or beneficiaries. RF-TL4-a-S (story-mining), RF-TL4-b-S (advocacy-vacuum), RF-C4-a-S (advocacy-as-warfare), RF-C4-b-S (advocacy-vacuum), RF-PM4-a-S (advocacy-overclaim), RF-PM4-b-S (instrumentalising volunteers), RF-D4-a-S (co-optation), RF-D4-b-S (voice-arrogation).
- **Employee-mechanic import on the ethics axis.** Applying staff ethics codes to volunteers without adaptation. RF-D1-a-S. Distinct from D4's employee-mechanic import (which is about authority tools) — this is about ethics-frame import specifically. Cross-references D4 §7.

### 7.2 Relational red-flag families (Axis R, canonical per design doc §D-5)

- **Boundary-blindness.** Narrating a dual relationship / gift / disclosure with no recognition that a boundary exists. RF-TL1-b-R, RF-TL3-b-R, RF-C3-b-R (relational-to-public leap), RF-D1-b-R, RF-PM2-c-R (disclosure without supervision channel), RF-PM3-b-R.
- **Boundary-as-wall.** Treating every relational tension as rule-enforcement; policing framed as the only tool. RF-PM2-b-R (blanket disclosure ban), RF-D3-b-R (register as opacity from the volunteer).
- **Exceptionalism.** "That rule is for others; my relationship with this volunteer is different." The most predictive relational flag in the taxonomy. RF-TL2-b-R (private-favour framing), RF-C1-b-R ("they're both adults"), RF-D3-c-R (no-register as virtue signal).

### 7.3 Cross-axis families

- **Emotional-calibration by in-group.** Warmth calibrated to volunteers who read as familiar; distance to those who don't. RF-C2-b-R (attention concentration), RF-D2-b-R (culture reading), RF-PM1-b-R (redesign warmth by tenure).

Each canonical relational family (boundary-blindness, boundary-as-wall, exceptionalism) is present in ≥1 flag per §5 acceptance criterion 3.
