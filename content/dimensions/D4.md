## 1. Dimension summary

D4 tests whether a practitioner can set expectations, hold volunteers accountable, and measure what volunteer effort actually accomplishes — **without importing employment mechanics that do not apply** and **without leaving the accountability tension unresolved by design**. Groble's (2018) Volunteer Authority Model names the load-bearing frame: organisations exercise authority over volunteers through identifiable tool categories (expectation-setting, role documentation, feedback structures, escalation paths, corrective action), not through supervisory command. D4 probes surface how consciously the practitioner works those tools at the level of their remit.

Post-pilot enhancements (post-pilot volunteer–staff conflict resolution literature per README) are **out of scope for T2-H**. They sharpen levelling after real pilot data, particularly around 4.4 corrective-action red-flag calibration.

## 2. Sub-domains (translation lattice)

| # | Sub-domain | What it tests | Primary literature anchors |
|---|---|---|---|
| 4.1 | Performance expectation-setting & role design | Whether expectations are set explicitly and matched to volunteer role scope before work begins | Groble (G1) — authority tools; Ellis — role clarity; Rochester — models of involvement |
| 4.2 | Feedback & performance conversations | Whether formative feedback is a standing practice, calibrated to volunteers (no employment leverage) | Groble (G1); Ellis — supervision as coaching; Grossman & Furano — feedback tied to motivation |
| 4.3 | Impact measurement & attribution | Whether volunteer effort is tied to program outcomes honestly, distinguishing outputs from outcomes | Rehnborg — volunteer economic value; Hager & Brudney — capacity/outcome studies; Brudney & Meijs (G3) |
| 4.4 | Accountability & corrective action | Whether accountability moves are calibrated (repair → redesign → exit) without employment-mechanic import; whether liability boundary is understood | Groble (G1); Groble & Brudney 2015 (G2) — VPA/liability; Ellis |

Each band draws 4 probes across these sub-domains, weighted by scope.

## 3. Scope-boundary-awareness rule (v1.0)

Applied framework-wide (see README §Two Scoring Axes) and lifted verbatim from D5 r1:

- **Substantive escalation protects the floor.** A practitioner who correctly recognises a probe is outside their remit and hands off to the right role earns credit at the developmental level of their recognition + handoff, not a penalty for not answering in-band.
- **Uncorrected miscalibration caps a sub-domain at Proficient.** A practitioner who confidently answers a probe substantially outside their scope without noticing they are outside it cannot exceed Proficient in that sub-domain regardless of Expert-level substance elsewhere.
- **Probes and whole sub-domains may thin or drop at lower bands.** Team Lead does not see org-wide accountability-policy probes; Director does not see hands-on individual corrective conversations. This is expected. Every band gets at least one probe per sub-domain except where scope makes the sub-domain nonsensical for the band (documented per-probe below).

## 4. Groble authority-tool reuse note (source-list integration)

Per `docs/D4-groble-corroboration.md`, the Groble Volunteer Authority Model is admitted to the D4 cumulative source list with three specific uses:

- **G1 Groble (2018) dissertation** anchors every probe touching volunteer accountability, performance conversations, or corrective action. Concretely: expectations, documentation, role design, feedback structures, escalation paths, and corrective action are the six practical tools D4 tests.
- **G2 Groble & Brudney (2015) NPPF** anchors 4.4 red-flag clauses distinguishing **accountability** (which stays with the volunteer for their conduct) from **liability** (which is governed by VPA and organisational policy, not by disciplining the volunteer).
- **G3 Brudney & Meijs (2019) NML** anchors Program Manager and Director probes where the frame shifts from individual authority to stewardship/program-design authority.

No new scoring code is needed for D4. The engine picks up D4 probes by sub-domain tag; strength markers, band floors, and red-flag caps operate identically to D1–D3 and D5.

## 5. Probes by audience band

Each probe is presented as: **Prompt · Sub-domain · Anchors · Developmental-level anchors (Foundational / Developing / Proficient / Expert) with strength markers · Red-flag / misconception clauses · Scope-boundary-awareness note.**

### 5.1 Team Lead — direct, hands-on leadership; evidence over vocabulary

The Team Lead assesses a small volunteer team. Probes focus on setting expectations one-to-one, giving corrective feedback face-to-face, understanding what the team's work contributes to program outcome, and handling a first-order accountability breach without reaching for HR framing. Org-wide policy on authority tools is **out of Team-Lead scope** and does not appear as a probe here.

#### Probe TL-1 — Setting expectations with a new volunteer (4.1 Performance expectation-setting & role design)

**Prompt.** A new volunteer, Danilo, is starting a weekly three-hour shift on a task another volunteer used to run. There is no written role description for the task; the previous volunteer just "knew what to do." Walk through the first conversation.

- **Anchors:** Groble (G1) — expectation-setting as first authority tool; Ellis — role clarity; Rochester — model-of-involvement fit.
- **Foundational (1.0–3.0).** Walks Danilo through the task by demonstration; expectations remain tacit. S1 evidence: gives Danilo a starting point, does not surface any explicit expectation.
- **Developing (3.1–5.5).** Names one or two things Danilo needs to do (arrival time, handover) but leaves the substantive work-quality expectations to be picked up. S1: partial explicit expectation, no shared referent.
- **Proficient (5.6–7.5).** Distinguishes (a) *what* the task delivers, (b) *what "done well" looks like*, (c) *what handover to the next shift requires*, (d) *what is not in scope* — writes at least a light note to close the tacit-knowledge gap. S2 evidence: names the risk of expectations-by-inheritance and articulates the tool (light role note) that removes it.
- **Expert (7.6–10.0).** Uses the moment to close the role-doc gap for the shift more broadly, not just for Danilo; frames the light role note as a shared team artefact; S3 evidence: has previously turned a tacit-expectation failure into a durable role artefact and can trace the effect on hand-over quality or churn.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-TL1-a.** Treats "walk-through by demo" as sufficient expectation-setting. Uncorrected: caps 4.1 at Proficient.
  - **RF-TL1-b.** Uses onboarding-checklist HR framing without adapting to volunteer-scope autonomy (Groble G1). Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap per §5.5.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. Designing the role-doc *system* for the team's shifts is Coordinator-plus; a Team Lead who correctly says "I'd flag the missing role docs to my coordinator" *while still handling this conversation* earns escalation credit for the systemic move.

#### Probe TL-2 — Corrective feedback to a slipping volunteer (4.2 Feedback & performance conversations)

**Prompt.** A volunteer, Rita, has been arriving late on her shift for three weeks and the quality of her handover notes has dropped. You have not raised it with her yet. Walk through the conversation and what precedes it.

- **Anchors:** Groble (G1) — feedback structures as authority tool without employment leverage; Ellis — supervision as coaching; Grossman & Furano — feedback tied to motivation.
- **Foundational.** Waits, hopes it lifts, mentions it in passing. S1: no structured move.
- **Developing.** Opens the conversation but frames it as an ask/apology ("I hate to say this, but…"); leaves the substantive expectation implicit. S1: raised, not resolved.
- **Proficient.** Prepares with the specifics (three dates late; two handover notes with named gaps); opens with observed behaviour, not attribution; asks what has changed for Rita before offering feedback; closes with a shared next step. S2: distinguishes formative feedback from disciplinary framing; names the volunteer-context adjustment.
- **Expert.** Treats the conversation as one instance of a standing feedback rhythm the team already has (not a special episode); recognises that a slipping volunteer may be a role-fit signal as well as a performance signal and holds both in view; S3 evidence: has previously changed a team feedback practice after a similar case and can name the effect.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-TL2-a.** Frames the conversation as a "warning" or "write-up." Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap (employee-mechanic import).
  - **RF-TL2-b.** Avoids the conversation because volunteers "cannot really be held to it." Uncorrected: 4.2 cap at Proficient (authority-vacuum misconception).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. Redesigning the team's feedback rhythm broadly is Coordinator-plus; a Team Lead handling this conversation and separately raising the rhythm gap earns escalation credit.

#### Probe TL-3 — What the team's work adds up to (4.3 Impact measurement & attribution)

**Prompt.** Your team's shift generates a set of activities every week (visits made, meals delivered, sessions run). A funder is asking your coordinator "so what did volunteers actually change?" Your coordinator asks you to say what you would say. Answer.

- **Anchors:** Rehnborg — volunteer economic value; Hager & Brudney (Balancing Act); Brudney & Meijs (G3) — stewardship framing.
- **Foundational.** Reports the activity count (hours, visits, meals). S1: output count, no outcome tie.
- **Developing.** Adds one or two beneficiary anecdotes to the activity count. S1: partial outcome framing, no mechanism.
- **Proficient.** Names the concrete outcome the team's shift is designed to change (e.g. isolated older adults report a week's meaningful contact; food-insecure households receive a week's groceries with dignity) and how the shift's activity is the mechanism for that outcome — while noting what the team cannot claim credit for (e.g. long-run health outcomes, poverty reduction). S2: separates outputs from outcomes; names the honest attribution boundary.
- **Expert.** Adds the counterfactual dimension ("without this shift, the outcome we can name would not happen at this cadence"); notes what evidence the team already has and what it does not; treats overclaim as a reputational risk that erodes future advocacy; S3 evidence: has previously refused to endorse an overclaim to a funder and can name the trade-off.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-TL3-a.** Reads hours or activity count as the outcome. Uncorrected: 4.3 cap at Proficient.
  - **RF-TL3-b.** Overclaims causal impact to look good to the funder (e.g. "we ended isolation for these people"). Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. Program-level attribution reporting is Program Manager scope; a Team Lead who correctly signals the caveat and hands the funder-facing framing up earns escalation credit.

#### Probe TL-4 — A first-order accountability breach (4.4 Accountability & corrective action)

**Prompt.** A volunteer, Jae, no-shows a shift for the second consecutive week without notice. Another volunteer had to cover both times. What do you do this week, and what is the ladder if it happens again?

- **Anchors:** Groble (G1) — corrective-action toolkit; Ellis — proportionality in supervision; Groble & Brudney (G2) — accountability vs. liability boundary.
- **Foundational.** Sends a mild reminder; drops it if Jae shows next week. S1: no calibrated response.
- **Developing.** Calls Jae, expresses disappointment, restates the rota expectation. S1: named, not laddered.
- **Proficient.** Opens with observable behaviour (two no-shows, cover impact on another volunteer); asks what changed; sets an explicit expectation with a shared next check-in; names the ladder — one more no-show without notice triggers a role-fit conversation, not a discipline conversation. S2: proportional; separates commitment failure from role-fit failure; names the tool (ladder) as the authority instrument, per Groble.
- **Expert.** Treats the no-show pattern as data about the role and the team as well as about Jae (is the rota over-committing volunteers; is notice hard because there's no easy channel); holds the individual conversation and the systemic read in the same move; S3 evidence: has previously changed a rota or notice practice after a similar pattern and can name the effect on no-show rate.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-TL4-a.** Reaches for a written warning, PIP, or "final warning" language. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap (employee-mechanic import).
  - **RF-TL4-b.** Treats the situation as "well, they're volunteers, we can't really do anything." Uncorrected: 4.4 cap at Proficient (authority-vacuum misconception).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. Formal off-boarding of a volunteer is Coordinator-plus; a Team Lead handling this ladder correctly and correctly signalling that an exit conversation would be a coordinator hand-off earns escalation credit at that point.

### 5.2 Coordinator — day-to-day management; operational proficiency

The Coordinator runs a volunteer pool across shifts and roles. Probes shift from a single volunteer to consistent role documentation across the pool, coaching team leads to hold the feedback conversation, cohort-level output-vs-outcome literacy, and designing a corrective-action ladder that is not PIP-import.

#### Probe C-1 — Role documentation across the pool (4.1 Performance expectation-setting & role design)

**Prompt.** Your volunteer pool covers eight distinct roles, some documented on a printout from 2019, some documented in a team lead's head, some not at all. The ED (Executive Director) wants "consistent role descriptions across the pool by next quarter." How do you approach it, and what do you refuse to do?

- **Anchors:** Groble (G1) — documentation as authority tool; Rochester — models-of-involvement fit; Ellis.
- **Foundational.** Writes a single template and asks each team lead to fill it in for their roles. S1: template compliance, no fitness read.
- **Developing.** Writes a template, differentiates it lightly by role type, gets partial adoption. S1: some model fit, no maintenance loop.
- **Proficient.** Distinguishes what has to be common across roles (mission-fit, safeguarding, minimum-time expectations) from what has to differ (task-specific "done well" criteria, escalation path); designs the template accordingly; sets a light maintenance rhythm so it does not decay by 2029. S2: names the authority-tool logic and the decay failure mode.
- **Expert.** Refuses to standardise the "done well" criteria across roles that Rochester's models make structurally different (a service-delivery role and a member-activist role cannot share the same performance frame); documents the refusal so it does not re-litigate; treats the role doc as living infrastructure, not a one-off deliverable; S3 evidence: has previously killed a false-standardisation ask and can name the effect on volunteer clarity or churn.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-C1-a.** Cargo-cults an HR job-description template onto volunteer roles. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap.
  - **RF-C1-b.** Treats role docs as a compliance artefact rather than an authority tool. Uncorrected: 4.1 cap at Proficient.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe C-2 — Coaching a team lead through a hard feedback conversation (4.2 Feedback & performance conversations)

**Prompt.** A team lead comes to you: their long-standing volunteer is doing the task the same way she's always done it, but the task has moved on. The team lead is avoiding the conversation because "she's been with us for eight years." Coach them.

- **Anchors:** Groble (G1) — feedback structures; Ellis — coaching frame; Grossman & Furano — recognition-and-feedback interaction.
- **Foundational.** Tells the team lead to just have the conversation. S1: prescription without preparation.
- **Developing.** Role-plays the opener with the team lead; leaves the substantive design to them. S1: partial coaching.
- **Proficient.** Helps the team lead separate three things — the substantive change in the task, the volunteer's long service (respected, not weaponised), and the coaching move (feedback is *because* of the long relationship, not despite it); prepares the team lead with specific observable examples; agrees a debrief. S2: named coaching moves; long-service reframe.
- **Expert.** Recognises that team-lead avoidance is the systemic signal (not the volunteer's tenure) and addresses the avoidance directly ("this conversation is hard because you have not had it before with her; that's the muscle to build, not the exception to make"); pairs the coaching with a light change to the team's feedback rhythm; S3 evidence: has previously changed a team's feedback cadence and can name the effect on avoidance.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-C2-a.** Frames tenure as a reason to avoid feedback. Uncorrected: red-flag cap (authority-vacuum, mis-applied to seniority).
  - **RF-C2-b.** Coaches the team lead into a scripted HR-style script. Uncorrected: 4.2 cap at Proficient.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe C-3 — Outputs, outcomes, and what to hand up (4.3 Impact measurement & attribution)

**Prompt.** Your Program Manager is preparing a mid-year report. She has asked you for "the impact number" for the volunteer pool for the first half. What do you give her, and what do you refuse to give her?

- **Anchors:** Rehnborg; Hager & Brudney; Brudney & Meijs (G3); Ellis.
- **Foundational.** Sends hours + activity counts. S1: output only.
- **Developing.** Adds a couple of case anecdotes. S1: mixed output-and-anecdote, no framing.
- **Proficient.** Sends (a) volunteer-effort metrics (hours, roles, retention), (b) the specific outcome the pool is *designed* to move (e.g. weekly meaningful contact for 220 households) with the pool's contribution to that outcome, (c) an explicit note on what the pool cannot claim credit for and what needs qualitative pairing. S2: separates output/outcome; names attribution honesty as a stewardship duty.
- **Expert.** Refuses to give a headline "impact number" that collapses outputs, outcomes, and attribution into one figure; explains to the PM that the compressed number will misrepresent in the board pack and offers a paired figure and paragraph instead; S3 evidence: has previously stopped a headline number from being published and can name what changed.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-C3-a.** Sends hours as "impact" without note or paragraph. Uncorrected: 4.3 cap at Proficient.
  - **RF-C3-b.** Manufactures a causal-impact figure to look good. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. Board-facing narrative is Program Manager and Director scope; a Coordinator who correctly hands up the paired figure with the caveats owns their band correctly.

#### Probe C-4 — A corrective-action ladder that is not a PIP (4.4 Accountability & corrective action)

**Prompt.** Design a corrective-action ladder for the volunteer pool. Explain each step, what triggers moving up a rung, and what stops the ladder from becoming an employment-mechanic import.

- **Anchors:** Groble (G1) — corrective-action toolkit; Groble & Brudney (G2) — accountability vs. liability; Ellis.
- **Foundational.** Adapts a written-warning / final-warning / termination ladder from an HR handbook. S1: unadapted import.
- **Developing.** Uses a softer ladder (chat / meeting / role change / exit) but does not name what stops it from becoming disciplinary in tone. S1: named, uncalibrated.
- **Proficient.** Ladder is: (i) informal check-in, observable behaviour + shared next step; (ii) role-fit conversation — is this the right role or task; (iii) role change or refresh of expectations; (iv) constructive exit if fit cannot be recovered. Trigger between rungs is *pattern + no change*, not a single incident. Names explicitly that the ladder is a fit ladder, not a discipline ladder; the exit is constructive, not termination. S2: names the mechanism that keeps the ladder inside Groble's authority-tool frame.
- **Expert.** Separates two ladders — a fit ladder (the above) and a **safeguarding/liability** ladder (immediate suspension, formal record, referral) — and is explicit that the two ladders operate on different signals and are not interchangeable; anchors the safeguarding ladder in G2 (Volunteer Protection Act / liability); S3 evidence: has previously stopped a fit-ladder move being used where a safeguarding-ladder move was needed, or vice versa, and can name the consequence.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-C4-a.** Single ladder that collapses fit issues into safeguarding-liability moves (or vice versa). Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap.
  - **RF-C4-b.** Adopts PIP / progressive-discipline vocabulary uncorrected. Uncorrected: red-flag cap (employee-mechanic import).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

### 5.3 Program Manager — cross-team oversight; developing strategic framing

The Program Manager operates above single-team detail and inside program design. Probes shift to role architecture across programs, feedback rhythm at scale, attribution/counterfactual thinking, and constructive-exit design at program level.

#### Probe PM-1 — Role architecture across programs (4.1 Performance expectation-setting & role design)

**Prompt.** You run three programs. Program A uses volunteers as service-delivery labour (fixed shifts, defined task); Program B uses volunteers as member-activists (self-organised, mission-driven); Program C uses episodic volunteers for disaster response. The board asks for "one volunteer role framework across the org." Respond.

- **Anchors:** Groble (G1); Rochester — four models of volunteer involvement; Brudney & Meijs (G3) — stewardship differentiation; Ellis.
- **Foundational.** Complies; writes one framework. S1: false standardisation.
- **Developing.** Pushes back weakly; ends up with one framework and light program-specific annexes. S1: partial pushback.
- **Proficient.** Distinguishes what is program-independent (mission-fit, safeguarding, minimum expectations, volunteer rights) from what is model-dependent (task definition, "done well" criteria, feedback rhythm, exit ladder); proposes a common **charter** and program-specific **role architectures** — same principle, different instrument. S2: names Rochester model separation as the design constraint.
- **Expert.** Explains to the board that a single framework across the three programs would produce a **worst-of-all-worlds** structure: too much oversight for member-activists, too little clarity for service-delivery volunteers, wrong cadence for episodic disaster response; anchors the argument in the Groble authority-tool logic; S3 evidence: has previously defended differentiated program-level role architecture at the board and won on the mechanism argument.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-PM1-a.** Treats a single framework as strategically neutral. Uncorrected: 4.1 cap at Proficient.
  - **RF-PM1-b.** Collapses under executive pressure without articulating the mechanism cost. Uncorrected: red-flag cap.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe PM-2 — Feedback rhythm at program scale (4.2 Feedback & performance conversations)

**Prompt.** Volunteer-feedback practice across your three programs is inconsistent: Program A has weekly team debriefs; Program B has none; Program C has a post-deployment retro. Coordinators are asking for a "common feedback framework." Design one.

- **Anchors:** Groble (G1) — feedback structures; Ellis — supervision as coaching; Grossman & Furano.
- **Foundational.** Mandates the same rhythm across programs. S1: false standardisation.
- **Developing.** Designs a light common rhythm plus program-specific additions; leaves the "what makes a feedback conversation" content generic. S1: rhythm without content.
- **Proficient.** Separates two design axes — **cadence** (how often, tied to program model — continuous for A, episodic-post-event for C, deliberate-onboarding-plus-ad-hoc for B) and **content** (what a feedback conversation looks like — same coaching frame across programs, calibrated observable-behaviour → shared-next-step); pairs cadence design to Rochester model. S2: named axes, tied to model-of-involvement.
- **Expert.** Adds a **feedback-avoidance monitor** — a light check that surfaces when a coordinator or team lead is quietly not holding the conversation; treats avoidance, not lack of framework, as the modal failure; S3 evidence: has previously moved a team from feedback-avoidance to feedback-holding and can name what changed.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-PM2-a.** Designs a framework that ignores program-model differences. Uncorrected: red-flag cap.
  - **RF-PM2-b.** Assumes providing the framework changes the practice (documentation ≠ practice). Uncorrected: 4.2 cap at Proficient.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe PM-3 — Attribution, counterfactual, and honest impact reporting (4.3 Impact measurement & attribution)

**Prompt.** The ED wants a headline "impact statement" for volunteers across your programs, to lead the annual report. What do you write, and what do you decline to write?

- **Anchors:** Rehnborg; Hager & Brudney; Brudney & Meijs (G3); Ellis; Groble & Brudney (G2) — accountability framing.
- **Foundational.** Writes generic celebratory language about "life-changing impact." S1: overclaim by default.
- **Developing.** Writes a mixed statement (some outcome language, some output count); leaves attribution implicit. S1: partial framing.
- **Proficient.** Writes a statement that (a) names the concrete outcomes volunteers contribute to, (b) names volunteer effort as one mechanism among several, (c) is defensible under a funder auditing the claim; declines to write a statement that treats hours as impact or that claims causal effect the data cannot support. S2: separates output/outcome, named counterfactual awareness.
- **Expert.** Frames overclaim as a **strategic risk** to the org's future funding and advocacy voice (funders remember overclaims; reputation-for-honesty is an asset); names what qualitative evidence would strengthen the statement and commits to it; S3 evidence: has previously reshaped an annual-report claim after a funder audit or peer challenge and can name the trust dividend.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-PM3-a.** Treats board/donor-facing overclaim as a small cost. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap.
  - **RF-PM3-b.** Confuses ambitious mission statement with substantiated impact statement. Uncorrected: 4.3 cap at Proficient.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe PM-4 — Constructive-exit design at program level (4.4 Accountability & corrective action)

**Prompt.** A long-standing volunteer in Program B is misaligned with the current program direction and it is not resolvable through role change. Design how you would end their volunteering relationship, and defend the design to a coordinator who thinks "we can't really ask a volunteer to leave."

- **Anchors:** Groble (G1); Groble & Brudney (G2); Ellis; Brudney & Meijs (G3).
- **Foundational.** Avoids the conversation; hopes the volunteer drifts. S1: authority-vacuum default.
- **Developing.** Asks the volunteer to leave; frames it apologetically without a shared narrative. S1: named, not designed.
- **Proficient.** Designs the exit as: (i) prepared conversation with observable behaviour + program-direction rationale, held by the right person; (ii) offered alternatives (referral to a partner org, alumni-status, event-only participation) that respect the volunteer's contribution; (iii) explicit thanks and closure; (iv) documented so it is not re-litigated. Answers the coordinator: yes we can, and *not doing so* is the deeper disrespect — it silently tells the volunteer they don't matter enough to have a real conversation with. S2: named mechanism; treats exit as an authority tool per Groble.
- **Expert.** Recognises that the volunteer's misalignment is legitimate — they've been on the program for years, the program has changed, this is not their failure — and designs the exit to preserve their reputation and future engagement with the sector; treats the exit as a **stewardship** move (G3) rather than a corrective one; S3 evidence: has previously designed a constructive exit and can name the volunteer's post-exit response.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-PM4-a.** Frames the exit as termination. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap.
  - **RF-PM4-b.** Treats "you cannot ask a volunteer to leave" as a settled rule. Uncorrected: 4.4 cap at Proficient (authority-vacuum misconception).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

### 5.4 Director — org-level design; advocacy; policy and infrastructure

The Director sets the philosophy for how the org exercises authority over volunteers, owns program-outcome honesty as a governance commitment, and designs the accountability infrastructure that keeps the front-line from having to reinvent it. Probes drop hands-on operational detail. Instead, they test policy, board narrative, cross-program consistency, and the accountability–liability boundary at governance level.

#### Probe D-1 — Org-level authority-tool policy (4.1 Performance expectation-setting & role design)

**Prompt.** Draft the two-paragraph section on volunteer expectations and role design for the org's next three-year plan, and defend it to a board member who wants "the same performance framework we use for staff, applied consistently across the org."

- **Anchors:** Groble (G1) — authority-tool policy at org level; Ellis; Brudney & Meijs (G3); Rochester.
- **Foundational.** Writes vague "volunteers are valued" prose; concedes to the board member. S1: convention, no position.
- **Developing.** Writes principled prose about volunteer distinctiveness but concedes on the "one framework" instrument. S1: half a position.
- **Proficient.** Distinguishes the **authority tools** the org will use for volunteers (expectation-setting, documentation, feedback, escalation, corrective action, exit) from the employment mechanics it will *not* import (PIP, progressive discipline, termination-as-first-tool); defends the distinction as **fit-for-purpose**, not soft — the volunteer authority relationship is real, structurally different, and requires its own toolkit; concedes governance guardrails (safeguarding, equal-treatment, complaint mechanism) without conceding the mechanism. S2: names Groble authority-tool logic at policy level.
- **Expert.** Positions volunteer authority-tool policy as a **strategic asset** (staff are not the volunteer's employer; treating them as such would attract legal risk and destroy the relationship); ties the position to the org's mission narrative for volunteers; anchors it in the research literature (Groble; Rochester; Brudney & Meijs); S3 evidence: names a governance mechanism (annual review of authority-tool coherence as part of program review) that keeps the policy honest and prevents HR-import drift.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-D1-a.** Imports the staff performance-management frame without adaptation. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap.
  - **RF-D1-b.** Positions volunteer distinctiveness as a soft/aspirational preference rather than a structural claim. Uncorrected: 4.1 cap at Proficient.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe D-2 — Feedback culture as governance (4.2 Feedback & performance conversations)

**Prompt.** A whistleblower-style volunteer complaint has surfaced that a program has been sitting on unresolved feedback for months. The board wants to know if this is a pattern. Design the governance response.

- **Anchors:** Groble (G1) — feedback structures; Ellis; Studer & von Schnurbein — organisational culture; Groble & Brudney (G2).
- **Foundational.** Treats the complaint as isolated; asks the program to resolve it. S1: no governance read.
- **Developing.** Commissions a program review; treats the complaint as a program problem. S1: partial move.
- **Proficient.** Frames the complaint as a **signal about the org's feedback infrastructure**, not just this program; commissions a light audit across programs of whether feedback conversations are being held, escalated, and closed; commits to reporting findings to the board with named follow-up. S2: signal → system move; named accountability upward.
- **Expert.** Uses the complaint as a governance moment to install a **standing measure** (e.g. a light quarterly audit of unresolved-feedback backlog by program) so the next such complaint surfaces earlier; treats feedback-holding as a governance capability, not just a program-manager competence; S3 evidence: has previously installed a standing measure that surfaced a systemic issue before it became a complaint.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-D2-a.** Frames the complaint as a personnel problem to be contained. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap.
  - **RF-D2-b.** Treats feedback culture as a soft variable outside board oversight. Uncorrected: 4.2 cap at Proficient.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe D-3 — Impact honesty as a strategic asset (4.3 Impact measurement & attribution)

**Prompt.** Present volunteer impact to the board. You have five minutes. You have one number and one paragraph. What do you use, and what do you deliberately not use?

- **Anchors:** Rehnborg; Hager & Brudney; Brudney & Meijs (G3); Ellis; Rogelberg — programme sustainability.
- **Foundational.** Presents hours or activity count as "impact." S1: output framed as outcome.
- **Developing.** Presents a mixed figure with vague causal language. S1: partial framing.
- **Proficient.** The number is an honest outcome metric with a stated attribution boundary (e.g. "220 food-insecure households received weekly groceries with dignity through volunteer effort this quarter; broader food-security outcomes are shaped by many factors beyond our program"); the paragraph names what the volunteer effort contributes as a mechanism and what it does not; deliberately does not use headline causal-impact language. S2: separated output/outcome + attribution; treats overclaim as a governance risk.
- **Expert.** Frames impact honesty as a **strategic asset** — funders, partners, and staff notice which orgs overclaim, and the org's advocacy voice on volunteer capacity has to be defensible for it to move policy; treats one honest number as more valuable than a headline number; S3 evidence: has previously reshaped a board-facing metric toward honesty and can name the reputational or funding return.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-D3-a.** Treats board-facing overclaim as low-cost because "boards want good news." Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap.
  - **RF-D3-b.** Confuses ambitious mission language with defensible impact statement. Uncorrected: 4.3 cap at Proficient.
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band.

#### Probe D-4 — Accountability, liability, and where the two must not be confused (4.4 Accountability & corrective action)

**Prompt.** A volunteer's driving in the course of program work has been involved in a road incident. Legal counsel is asking about liability; the coordinator is asking about "consequences for the volunteer." The board is asking how the org will respond publicly. Take a position and act.

- **Anchors:** Groble & Brudney (G2) — VPA / liability boundary conditions; Groble (G1) — authority-tool toolkit; Ellis; Studer & von Schnurbein.
- **Foundational.** Conflates the two threads; either disciplines the volunteer as if they were an employee, or drops accountability entirely because "they're a volunteer, the org is liable." S1: unresolved.
- **Developing.** Separates the threads but handles both weakly; the volunteer conversation is not held, the legal conversation is over-conceded. S1: named, uncalibrated.
- **Proficient.** Explicitly separates (a) the **liability** question (governed by the Volunteer Protection Act framework, insurance, and org policy — a legal-and-policy conversation with counsel), (b) the **accountability** question with the volunteer (a fit-and-safeguarding conversation about whether driving in program work remains the right assignment), and (c) the **public narrative** (honest, dignified, does not scapegoat the volunteer, does not overclaim the org's oversight). Does not allow one thread to collapse into another. S2: names the G2 boundary explicitly.
- **Expert.** Uses the incident as a governance moment to review whether the org's authority-tool policy adequately covers program work with liability exposure (safeguarding, insurance, driver policy, incident reporting); ties the review to G2's mapping of VPA immunity limits; frames the volunteer's dignity and the org's legal duty as compatible constraints rather than tensions to be traded; S3 evidence: has previously handled a comparable incident and can name what the org changed.
- **Red flags / misconceptions.**
  - **RF-D4-a.** Scapegoats the volunteer to protect the org publicly. Uncorrected clear instance → red-flag cap (accountability-liability confusion, per G2).
  - **RF-D4-b.** Treats VPA immunity as a general shield that removes the need for org-level accountability. Uncorrected: red-flag cap (VPA-immunity misconception, per G2).
- **Scope-boundary-awareness.** In-band. This probe is Director-owned because it requires policy authority, board-facing communication, and legal-boundary judgement in the same move.

## 7. Red-flag / misconception summary

Three families run across the 16 probes and gate the sub-domain score when uncorrected:

- **Employee-mechanic import.** Reaching for PIP, progressive discipline, "written warning," "final warning," or termination language when the situation calls for a Groble authority-tool response. Present in RF-TL2-a, RF-TL4-a, RF-C1-a, RF-C4-b, RF-PM4-a, RF-D1-a. This is D4's counterpart to D5's employee-import family — kept distinct: D4 = authority/discipline import, D5 = recognition/retention import.
- **Authority-vacuum.** Failing to exercise any authority tool because "they're volunteers, we can't really." Present in RF-TL2-b, RF-TL4-b, RF-C2-a, RF-PM4-b. Groble names this as the mirror failure of employment-mechanic import — the org that avoids the conversation entirely fails the volunteer as squarely as the org that misapplies HR mechanics.
- **Accountability–liability confusion.** Collapsing the volunteer-conduct question and the organisational-liability question into a single move (either direction — scapegoating the volunteer to protect the org, or invoking VPA immunity to duck accountability). Present in RF-C4-a, RF-D4-a, RF-D4-b. Anchored specifically in Groble & Brudney (G2).

A fourth pattern — **impact overclaim** — appears at 4.3 across bands (RF-TL3-b, RF-C3-b, RF-PM3-a, RF-D3-a). It is the impact-side analogue of the accountability-side scapegoating pattern: both are ways of externalising an uncomfortable truth about program limits.
